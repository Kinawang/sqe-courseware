📖 Part 1: What is Misrepresentation?

Misrepresentation (虚假陈述): An unambiguous, false statement of fact (or law) addressed to the representee that induces the representee to enter into the contract.

Distinction from Terms

Misrepresentations are pre-contractual statements that are NOT incorporated into the contract as terms. If a statement IS a term, the contract is breached, and the terms rules apply (not misrepresentation).

Separate regimes:

  • B2B (Business to Business): Common law + Misrepresentation Act 1967 (this chapter's focus)
  • B2C (Business to Consumer): Consumer Rights Act 2015 s50 (services) + Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 (goods). These treat trader statements as terms, not misrepresentations.

📖 Part 2: The Five Elements

To establish a misrepresentation, the representee must prove FIVE elements:

  1. The statement was unambiguous
  2. The statement was a statement of fact or law (not opinion/intention)
  3. The statement was false (false when contract concluded)
  4. The statement was addressed to the representee
  5. The statement induced the contract (four sub-tests)

Element 1: Unambiguous

The test is objective — how would a reasonable person interpret the statement?

If a statement is ambiguous or unclear, it should be given the meaning that:

  • The representor intended, OR
  • The representor knew the representee would understand.

A representee cannot claim that a true statement is a misrepresentation simply because he interpreted it unreasonably.

Element 2: Statement (Words, Conduct, or Silence)

The requirement for a "statement" covers words communicated to the representee. But a misrepresentation can also be made by:

MethodCaseHeld
Conduct (行为)Gordon v Selico (1986)Seller painted over dry rot to hide it. This conduct conveyed a false impression that there was no dry rot — misrepresentation by conduct.
Not the whole truth (未披露全部事实)Dimmock v Hallett (1866-67)Seller described property as "let to Prudential at £800,000/yr" but failed to disclose that Prudential had given notice to end the rental. The half-truth misled the buyer — misrepresentation.
Silence (沉默)General ruleThere is no general duty of disclosure under English law. A party is not obliged to disclose all relevant facts.
⚠️ Exam Warning: Although silence generally does NOT amount to misrepresentation, watch for scenarios where one party knows more than the other but fails to disclose the full story, or behaves in a way that does not disclose the full story — these CAN be misrepresentations.

Special Case: Change of Circumstances

A statement that is true when made can become a misrepresentation if it becomes false before the contract is concluded. The representor is under a duty to correct the statement.

With v O'Flanagan [1936] Ch 575 CA
Facts: Seller told buyer the medical practice generated £50,000/year. This was true at the time. Before the sale, the income fell to £20,000/year. The seller did not correct the statement.
Held: The seller was under a duty to correct the statement. Failure to do so = misrepresentation.

Element 3: Statements of Fact or Law

The general position: a misrepresentation must be a statement of fact or law, NOT of opinion, belief, or intention.

TypeGeneral RuleException
Opinion / Belief (意见/信念)❌ NOT misrepresentation if representor is NOT in a better position to know the facts.✅ IS misrepresentation if representor IS in a better position to know the facts (because opinions imply knowledge of underlying facts).
Intention (意图)❌ NOT misrepresentation (statement of present intention).✅ IS misrepresentation if the representor fraudulently claims an intention he does not have (treated as false statement of fact).
Bisset v Wilkinson [1927] AC 177 (PC, NZ)
Facts: Seller said the land could support 2,000 sheep. Buyer knew the land had never been used as a sheep farm.
Held: NOT a misrepresentation. The seller was NOT in a better position to know — the statement was a statement of opinion, not fact.
Smith v Land & House Property Corporation (1884)
Facts: Hotel chain described a tenant as a "most desirable tenant". The tenant was actually behind with her rent.
Held: IS a misrepresentation. The seller (hotel chain) was in a better position to know the facts about the tenant, so the opinion implied a statement of fact.
Edgington v Fitzmaurice (1885)
Facts: Company said it was issuing shares to grow the business. In fact, the money was needed to pay off liabilities.
Held: IS a misrepresentation. A fraudulent statement of present intention is treated as a false statement of fact.
⚠️ Exam Warning: Look carefully for words indicating belief or intention — these lead to the assumption that the statement is opinion/belief (not misrepresentation by default). THEN check: (1) Is the representor in a better position to know the underlying facts? (2) Is the representor falsely stating an intention? If yes to either → it IS a misrepresentation.

📖 Part 3: The Four Tests for Inducement

Once the first three elements are established, the representee must prove that the statement induced the contract. The representee must satisfy FOUR conditions:

  1. The statement must be material (重要的)
  2. The representee must be aware of the statement
  3. The representor must intend the representee to act on the statement
  4. The representee must act on the statement

Test 1: Material

A statement is material if it would affect the judgment of a reasonable person in deciding whether to enter into the contract, or one that would induce him to enter without making such enquiries as he would otherwise make.

Exam Tip: If the scenario shows a buyer asking "is this in good condition?" and the seller answers "yes" and the buyer then buys → the statement is material. The scenario itself usually gives you the answer.

Test 2: Representee is Aware

The person must be aware of the misrepresentation before or at the time of contracting. If a person buys a damaged vase made to look perfect without inspecting it, the buyer was NOT aware — and cannot rescind.

Practice Example (from textbook, Question 3):
Facts: Shopkeeper paints over a damaged gun barrel, puts the gun in a wooden case. A woman in a hurry points to the case from across the room and buys it. When she opens the case, she sees the defect.
Held: She CANNOT rescind — she was not aware of the misrepresentation when she bought the gun.

Test 3: Representor Intends Representee to Act on It

The representor must have intended the representee to act on the statement. Statements made to specific people do NOT extend to random overhearers.

Revision Tip (from textbook):
Example: A seller tells a prospective buyer in a car showroom that a car is in good condition. A random person using the toilet overhears. The random person cannot claim misrepresentation — the representor was unaware of the random person and did not intend the statement for them.

Test 4: Representee Acts on the Statement

There must be a link between the statement and the action. Ask: would the representee have acted as he did but for the statement?

"Material contribution" test: Even if the statement was NOT the sole reason, the representee may still claim if the statement made a material contribution (played a real and substantial part) in his decision.

Factors That Undermine Inducement

FactorEffect
Representee fully relies on his own investigations❌ Undermines inducement — he did NOT act on the statement.
Representee fails to test accuracy (when offered opportunity)⚠️ Can still claim, but damages may be reduced for contributory negligence.
Exam Tip: The material contribution test makes inducement easier to prove than the "sole cause" test. SQE1-style questions often hinge on whether the representee had some reliance on the statement.

📖 Part 4: The Three Categories of Misrepresentation

CategoryWhat the Representee Must ProveRemedies
Fraudulent (欺诈性)Representor knew the statement was false, OR did not believe it was true, OR was reckless (not caring whether true or false).Rescission + Damages (all direct loss, regardless of foreseeability)
Negligent (疏忽性) — Common LawSpecial relationship + duty of care (tort of negligence).Rescission + Damages (all direct loss if reasonably foreseeable)
Negligent — MA 1967Once statement is false, the representor must prove he had reasonable grounds to make the statement + believed it was true at the time of contracting.Rescission + Damages (all direct loss)
Innocent (无辜性)Statement was not fraudulent or negligent. Representor had reasonable grounds to make the statement.Rescission. Damages at court's discretion under MA 1967 s2(2).

Key Distinction: Common Law vs. MA 1967 for Negligent Misrepresentation

AspectCommon Law (Tort)Misrepresentation Act 1967
Burden of ProofRepresentee must prove special relationship + duty of care + breach.Representor must prove he had reasonable grounds to believe the statement was true.
AdvantagesCan claim against a person who is not a party to the contract (if duty of care exists).Easier to prove for the representee — burden shifts to representor.

Damages — Comparison

CategoryScope of DamagesStandard
FraudulentAll direct loss flowing from the transactionRegardless of foreseeability
Negligent (both Common Law and MA 1967)All direct loss flowing from the transactionProvided reasonably foreseeable
InnocentNo automatic right. Court's discretion under MA 1967 s2(2).Court may award damages instead of rescission.
⚠️ Exam Warning: The aim of damages for ALL categories is the same: to put the representee in the position he would have been in if the contract had not been made. (This is the tort measure, not the contract measure — different from breach of contract damages which aim to put the party in the position if the contract had been performed.)

📖 Part 5: Rescission and Barriers

Rescission (撤销合同)

Rescission unwinds the contract — parties are returned to their pre-contract position. It is available as a remedy for all three categories of misrepresentation.

When is Rescission Unavailable?

Three Key Reasons Rescission is Unavailable:
BarrierExample
Affirmation (追认)Representee, being aware of the misrepresentation and right to rescind, communicates to the representor his intention to continue with the contract (through actions or words). Examples: continuing to hold shares bought on basis of misrepresentation; continuing to use a defective vehicle.
Destruction of Subject Matter (标的物灭失)Representee has demolished buildings acquired on the basis of a misrepresentation.
Third Party Rights (第三方权利)Goods acquired on the basis of misrepresentation have passed under contract to an innocent third party without notice.

Damages Instead of Rescission (Innocent Misrepresentation)

For innocent misrepresentation, the court has discretion under MA 1967 s2(2) to award damages instead of rescission. This is exercised when rescission would be a disproportionately severe remedy and damages would be adequate.

William Sindall plc v Cambridgeshire County Council [1994] 1 WLR 1016
Facts: Land sold for £3M with misrepresentation that nothing hindered development. Developer found a colony of newts costing £2,000 to rehome.
Held: Court exercised its discretion to award damages instead of rescission, considering:
  • The £2,000 cost was of minor importance compared to the £3M purchase price
  • The loss that could be caused to the buyer if the contract were not rescinded
  • The loss that would be caused to the county council by rescission
Principle: Rescission is a very strong remedy. The court may consider it disproportionate compared to the effect of the misrepresentation.

📋 Chapter Summary: Misrepresentation

  1. Definition: Unambiguous, false statement of fact/law that induces the contract.
  2. Five elements: Unambiguous + Statement of fact (not opinion) + False + Addressed to representee + Induced the contract.
  3. Three categories: Fraudulent (knew/reckless) / Negligent (no reasonable grounds) / Innocent (had reasonable grounds).
  4. Remedies: Rescission (all three) + Damages (fraudulent: all direct loss; negligent: reasonably foreseeable; innocent: discretionary).
  5. Barriers to rescission: Affirmation, destruction, third party rights.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: What are the three categories of misrepresentation and what distinguishes them?
Fraudulent: Representor knew statement was false / did not believe it / was reckless. Negligent: Representor did NOT have reasonable grounds to believe the statement was true (was careless). Innocent: Representor HAD reasonable grounds to believe the statement was true. Key difference: fraudulent = intentional dishonesty; negligent = carelessness; innocent = had reasonable basis.
Q2: A seller paints over a damaged gun barrel and puts it in a wooden case. The buyer in a hurry points to the case from across the room and buys it without opening it. Can the buyer rescind?
NO — the buyer was NOT aware of the misrepresentation when she bought the gun. She had not inspected it. A key test for inducement is that the representee must be AWARE of the misrepresentation at or before the time of contracting. Without awareness, the misrepresentation did not "induce" the contract. (This is the fact pattern of textbook Question 3.)
Q3: A pop group sponsors a tour. All five group members participate in promotional activities, but they all know the fifth member is not planning to participate. The company discovers this in June and wants to rescind. Can they?
YES — the pop group's conduct (participating in promotional activities as a group of five) amounted to a misrepresentation by conduct that all five would be on the tour. This misrepresentation induced the sponsorship contract. The remedy of rescission is available for ALL types of misrepresentation. (Correct answer: D in the textbook multiple choice.)
Q4: An art gallery owner tells a man a painting is by a famous artist (it is not — it was painted by her baby). The man takes a photograph and asks an art expert who confirms it is by the famous artist. The man buys the painting. Can he rescind?
YES — this is fraudulent misrepresentation (the woman was dishonest). A representee's own investigations do NOT undermine his ability to claim if the misrepresentation was fraudulent. Rescission is available. (Correct answer: B in the textbook multiple choice.)
Q5: A landowner told a developer there was nothing on a £3M land plot that would hinder development. The developer found newts costing £2,000 to rehome. Can the developer rescind?
Although rescission is theoretically available for innocent misrepresentation, the court is very likely to exercise its discretion under MA 1967 s2(2) to award damages instead of rescission. The £2,000 cost is of minor importance compared to the £3M purchase price — rescission would be a disproportionately severe remedy. (Correct answer: C — Sindall [1994] principle.)
Q6: A transport company buys a lorry after being told it's in good condition. After discovering faults, the seller pays half the repair costs. The company uses the lorry a second time, and it breaks down again. Can the company rescind?
NO — using the lorry for a second time after knowing the misrepresentation amounts to affirmation. Affirmation can be by actions (not just words). Once the representee knows the truth and acts inconsistently with rescission, the right to rescind is lost. (Correct answer: D in the textbook multiple choice.)
Q7: What is the difference between negligent misrepresentation at common law and under the Misrepresentation Act 1967?
Common Law (tort of negligence): Representee must prove a special relationship + duty of care + breach. Advantage: can claim against a person who is NOT party to the contract (if duty of care exists). MA 1967 (statute): Once statement is shown to be false, the BURDEN shifts to the representor to prove he had reasonable grounds to believe the statement was true. Easier for the representee.
Q8: Does silence (failing to disclose facts) amount to misrepresentation?
Generally NO — there is NO general duty of disclosure under English law. However, there are exceptions: (1) Change of circumstances (With v O'Flanagan) — if a true statement becomes false, the representor must correct it. (2) Not the whole truth (Dimmock v Hallett) — a half-truth that misleads can be a misrepresentation.
Q9: What damages are available for fraudulent misrepresentation? How does this differ from negligent?
Fraudulent: All direct loss flowing from the transaction, regardless of foreseeability. The aim: put the representee in the position he would have been in if the contract had not been made. Negligent: All direct loss, but provided it was reasonably foreseeable. Fraudulent misrepresentation gives broader damages because of the representor's dishonesty.
Q10: What are the three key reasons that make rescission unavailable?
(1) Affirmation — the representee, knowing of the misrepresentation and the right to rescind, communicates (by actions or words) his intention to continue with the contract. (2) Destruction of subject matter — the representee has destroyed the subject of the contract (e.g., demolished buildings). (3) Third party rights — goods have passed to an innocent third party without notice.
Q11: A farmer says his land can support 2,000 sheep. The buyer knows the land has never been used for sheep farming. Is this a misrepresentation?
NO — based on Bisset v Wilkinson [1927]. The seller was NOT in a better position to know the facts. The statement was an opinion, not a fact. The buyer knew the land had never been used as a sheep farm, so the seller could not have superior knowledge about sheep-farming capacity. Statements of opinion are not misrepresentation UNLESS the representor is in a better position to know the facts.
Q12: A company says it is issuing shares to grow the business, but in fact the money is to pay off liabilities. Is this a misrepresentation?
YES — based on Edgington v Fitzmaurice (1885). A fraudulent statement of present intention is treated as a false statement of fact. The company did NOT have the intention it claimed to have. This is fraudulent misrepresentation — the representor fraudulently claimed an intention he did not have.
