Part 1: Processions and Assemblies

SQE1 requires you to distinguish between processions and assemblies. Processions are considered a greater threat to public order and are therefore more strictly regulated.

1. Public Processions (公共游行) — POA 1986 ss 11–13

There is no statutory definition of "public procession", but logically it must involve more than one person moving along a route. It must be in a place to which the public has access or any highway.

Notification requirement (s 11):

  • Organisers must give written notice to a police station at least six days in advance, giving the date, time, route and names of organisers
  • Applies to processions demonstrating support/opposition, publicising a cause, or commemorating an event
  • Not required if: not reasonably practicable, or it is a common/customary event, or a funeral
R (Kay) v Commissioner of Police of the Metropolis [2008] UKHL 69
A mass protest cycle ride in London was a public procession, but as it was held monthly, it had become customary and did not have to be notified to the police.

Police powers to impose conditions (s 12):

  • If the senior officer of police reasonably believes the procession may result in:
    • serious public disorder
    • serious damage to property
    • serious disruption to the life of the community
    • or that the purpose is to intimidate others
  • The officer may give directions including conditions on the route or restrictions on entering any public place

Power to ban processions (s 13):

  • If the chief officer of police reasonably believes the s 12 powers will not be sufficient, he/she shall apply to the local council for an order prohibiting all public processions in the district (or part) for a maximum of three months
  • The consent of the Secretary of State for Home Affairs is required
  • In London, the Commissioner of Police applies directly to the Secretary of State
⚠️ Exam trap: Who bans a procession?
Three bodies must agree: chief officer of police + local council + Secretary of State. The police alone cannot ban — they can only impose conditions.

Offence: It is an offence to fail to notify or to disobey police conditions.

2. Public Assemblies (公共集会) — POA 1986 s 14

A public assembly is defined as "an assembly of two or more persons in a public place which is wholly or partly open to the air".

Police powers:

  • If the senior officer reasonably believes the same thresholds (serious disorder, damage, disruption, intimidation), the officer may impose conditions on:
    • the place the assembly is held
    • its maximum duration
    • the maximum number of persons
  • It is an offence to disobey police conditions
Exam Tip: No power to ban all assemblies!
Unlike processions, there is no power to ban all assemblies in the district. The police can only control individual assemblies.
R (Jones) v Commissioner of Police of the Metropolis (Extinction Rebellion) [2019] EWHC 2957 (Admin)

4. Trespassory Assemblies (非法侵入性集会) — CJPOA 1994

A trespassory assembly requires:

  • 20 or more persons on land in the open air
  • Land to which the public has no or only limited right of access
  • Likely that the occupier will not have given permission
  • May result in serious disruption to the life of the community or significant damage to land, buildings or monuments of historical/architectural/archaeological/scientific importance

Banning power: Chief officer applies to the council; Secretary of State must consent. Order may not exceed 4 days or a 5-mile radius from a specified centre.

DPP v Jones (Margaret) and Another [1999] 2 AC 240
21 people standing on the roadside verge adjacent to Stonehenge's perimeter fence, protesting about a banned festival. They were acquitted because they were not trespassing — their use of the highway was reasonable, there was no obstruction, and there is a right of peaceful assembly on the highway.
Practice Example 6.1
A protest group plans to hold meetings at 20 underground stations in London. Can the police stop these meetings?
No. These are public assemblies (not processions). There is no power to ban all the meetings. Police could attend each meeting individually and use their powers to control assemblies there.

5. Raves (狂欢派对) — CJPOA 1994

A police superintendent has the power to disperse a rave and stop persons travelling to a rave within 5 miles. Police may also seize vehicles and sound equipment.

A rave is defined as:

  • A gathering of 20 or more persons on land in the open air without an entertainment licence (if in a building, they must be trespassers)
  • Amplified music is played during the night
  • The loudness and duration is likely to cause serious distress to inhabitants of the locality
📋 Part 1 Summary
  • Processions (moving) → more strictly regulated; must notify; can be banned district-wide
  • Assemblies (static, 2+ people, open air) → less regulated; no duty to notify; cannot be banned district-wide
  • Trespassory assemblies (20+ on private land) → can be banned (4 days / 5-mile radius)
  • Raves (20+ with loud music at night, no licence) → police can disperse and seize equipment

Part 2: Breach of the Peace (破坏治安)

Key Term: Breach of the Peace
An ancient common-law concept. All citizens and particularly police officers have a duty to maintain the peace of the realm. Breach of the peace involves violence or a threat of violence, including provoking others to violence.

1. Definition

Two aspects to the modern definition:

  1. R v Howell (Erroll) [1982]: "An act done or threatened to be done which actually harms a person, or in his presence his property, or is likely to cause such harm or which puts someone in fear of such harm being done"
  2. Conduct that has the natural consequence of provoking others to violence
⚠️ Breach of the peace = violence or threat of violence, NOT noise or anti-social behaviour!
This is a frequent exam trap. Excessive noise alone is NOT breach of the peace.

2. Preventative Action

Moss v McLachlan [1985]
"Provided the police honestly and reasonably formed the opinion that there was a real risk of a breach of the peace in the sense that it was in close proximity both in place and time, then the conditions existed for reasonable preventative action."

3. Police Powers

The police may do anything reasonable to prevent a breach of the peace:

  • Power to ban a procession
  • Power to disperse an assembly
  • Power to enter private premises
  • Power to detain people
  • Power to prevent people travelling
  • Power to remove provocative signs or emblems
Exam Tip: Statutory vs Common-law powers
Breach of the peace gives police powers that overlap with public order legislation. In exam scenarios, identify whether the question is about the statutory position (POA 1986 / CJPOA 1994) or the common-law position (breach of the peace).

4. Human Rights Limits on Breach of the Peace

CaseFactsHolding
Redmond-Bate v DPP [2000]Christian fundamentalists preaching attracted a hostile crowd; police arrested the speakers when they refused to stopArrest unlawful. Speakers had Art 10 ECHR right to free expression. Speech was lawful even if offensive — it did not tend to provoke violence. Police should have dealt with the violent crowd members instead.
R (Laporte) v Chief Constable of Gloucestershire [2007]Police stopped coaches travelling from London to protest at an airbaseUnlawful. Not enough evidence that violence was imminent. Police cannot pre-emptively stop travel to protest.
Austin v Commissioner of Police for the Metropolis [2009] UKHL 53,000 people (including non-protesters) detained for 7 hours ("kettling")Lawful. Police action was reasonable and proportionate, justifying restriction of Art 5 ECHR (right to liberty). Mass detention = "kettling" is permissible when proportionate.
Practice Example 6.2
A large anti-war protest is organised in City Hall (indoors). Police fear opponents will disrupt it. What powers do they have?
This is not a public assembly under POA 1986 (it is indoors), but police could use their breach of the peace powers to enter the hall. They should not stop the meeting but should attempt to control the potentially violent opponents.

5. Enforcement

Breach of the peace is NOT a crime, although it carries a power of arrest. The arrested may be freed without charge.

Binding Over (具结保证)

A civil remedy with a criminal standard of proof (beyond reasonable doubt):

  • The court must be satisfied that a breach of the peace has occurred AND there is a real risk of violence in the future
  • The court must identify the specific conduct the individual must refrain from
  • Usually does not exceed 12 months
  • Defendant may be asked to pay a specified sum if they break the order (a recognisance)
  • If they refuse to be bound over, they can be imprisoned for up to 6 months
Percy v DPP [1995]
Defendant repeatedly climbed the fence into a military air base to protest. She was not violent and there was no evidence her actions would provoke trained military personnel to a violent response. No grounds to bind her over.

Obstruction of a Police Officer — s 89 Police Act 1996

If a person refuses the reasonable direction of a police officer legitimately trying to prevent a breach of the peace, they commit the offence of obstructing a constable in the execution of their duty.

Duncan v Jones [1936]
Defendants convicted for refusing to disperse a public meeting when a police officer asked.

Obstruction of the Highway — s 137 Highways Act 1980

  • The normal use of the highway is for passage and repassage
  • Wilful obstruction without lawful excuse is an offence
  • However, small protests on the highway are lawful if the highway is not actually blocked — there is a right to protest: DPP v Jones (Margaret) [1999]
📋 Part 2 Summary
  • Breach of the peace = common-law power; violence or threats of violence
  • Police may take any reasonable step to prevent imminent violence
  • HRA 1998 limits: Art 10 (expression) and Art 5 (liberty) constrain police — but "kettling" can be proportionate (Austin)
  • Enforcement: binding over (civil, criminal standard), obstruction of police officer, obstruction of highway

Part 3: Public Order Offences (POA 1986 ss 1–5)

Sections 1–5 of the POA 1986 create five criminal offences directed at public order, from the most serious (riot) to the least (harassment, alarm or distress). All may be committed in a public or private place and intent (mens rea) must be proved for each.

1. Riot (暴乱) — s 1

All elements must be proved:

ElementDetail
1 12 or more persons present togetherThe group must be using or threatening unlawful violence
2 Use or threaten unlawful violenceViolence to persons or property; includes throwing missiles
3 For a common purposeNeed not be pre-planned; requires the 12 to support each other in the violence/threats
4 Cause a person of reasonable firmness to fear for safetyObjective standard — no need to prove someone was actually scared
5 Using (not just threatening) unlawful violence for common purposeDefendants must actually use violence, not merely threaten it

Maximum sentence: 10 years' imprisonment.

2. Violent Disorder (暴力骚乱) — s 2

Similar to riot but:

  • Requires only 3 or more persons (not 12)
  • Does not require a common purpose
  • Committed by those who use OR threaten violence
  • Must still cause a person of reasonable firmness to fear for safety
R v Fleming [1989]
Two defendants could not be convicted of violent disorder (requires at least 3 persons). But they could be convicted of affray.

3. Affray (滋事) — s 3

  • One or two persons acting together
  • Use or threaten unlawful violence — cannot be words alone
  • Must cause a person of reasonable firmness present at the scene to fear for safety
R v Sanchez [1996]
Affray envisages at least 3 people: the person committing violence, their victim, and a bystander. The court considers all circumstances to determine whether it was reasonable for a bystander to be fearful.

Practice Example 6.3
After a football match, disgruntled supporters smash shop windows and loot contents. Which offence?
Riot is difficult — need 12+ acting for a common purpose; separate groups may not qualify. Affray doesn't cover property damage. Violent disorder is the best fit: only 3+ needed, no common purpose required, and it covers violence to property.

4. Fear or Provocation of Violence — s 4

Two elements:

  1. A person uses threatening, abusive or insulting words, behaviour, writing, sign or other visible representation
  2. With intent to:
    • cause that person to believe unlawful violence will be used against them, OR
    • provoke the immediate use of unlawful violence
"Threatening, abusive or insulting" has no technical legal meaning — it is what the ordinary person would understand by those words. Provocation need not be instantaneous but requires proximity in time and causation: R v Horseferry Road Justices [1991].

5. Harassment, Alarm or Distress (骚扰、惊恐或困扰) — s 5

Two elements:

  1. A person uses:
    • threatening, abusive or insulting words or behaviour, OR
    • threatening, abusive or insulting writing, sign or other visible representation, OR
    • disorderly behaviour
  2. Within the hearing or sight of a person likely to be caused harassment, alarm or distress
⚠️ s 5 is the ONLY offence that includes "disorderly behaviour" as a standalone category (no need for threatening/abusive/insulting words)!
DPP v Orum [1988]
Swearing at police officers is unlikely to be an offence under s 5 — they are used to it.
Percy v DPP [2002]
Defacing the US flag might be insulting to US service personnel, but the Art 10 ECHR right of free expression outweighs this.
Practice Example 6.4
A man follows a woman home at night, shouting and making indecent suggestions. She asks him to stop but he persists. Offence?
Yes — s 5 POA 1986 (causing harassment, alarm or distress). His words could be threatening, abusive or insulting, and his behaviour disorderly. It would be reasonable for the woman to feel harassed.

6. Aggravated Trespass (加重侵入) — CJPOA 1994 s 68

  • An offence to trespass on land with intent to disrupt, obstruct or intimidate those engaged in a lawful activity
  • A senior officer may direct those committing (or about to commit) this offence to leave the land
  • Prosecution must prove defendants were not just trespassing, but engaged in acts to disrupt lawful activity
R v Jones [2006] UKHL 16
Defendants entered RAF Fairford intending to damage aircraft, arguing that preparing weapons for an aggressive war in Iraq was not a "lawful activity" under international law. The House of Lords ruled that under UK law it was lawful — therefore the defendants had no defence.
📋 Part 3 Summary — The Numbers Game
  • Riot: 12+ persons, common purpose, must use violence → 10 years
  • Violent disorder: 3+ persons, no common purpose, use or threaten → 5 years
  • Affray: 1–2 persons, cannot be words alone → 3 years
  • s 4 Fear/provocation: threatening/abusive/insulting words + intent to cause fear/provoke violence
  • s 5 Harassment/alarm/distress: includes "disorderly behaviour"; likely to cause distress (not actual)
  • Aggravated trespass: trespass + intent to disrupt lawful activity

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: A man plans a public procession but has not notified the police. What is the legal position?
Failure to notify is an offence that may be committed by the organiser. It does NOT give police the power to ban the procession or impose conditions — those powers depend on the senior officer reasonably believing the procession may cause serious disorder, damage, disruption or intimidation. (Answer: E — prosecution for an offence)
Q2: Police stop football supporters from walking to the station after a match, telling them to wait 1 hour for rival supporters to disperse. A man refuses and is arrested. On what basis?
Breach of the peace. Previous violence makes it reasonable to believe violence might recur. Police may take reasonable preventative steps, including temporarily detaining people, and arrest those who refuse. (Answer: B)
Q3: Two drunk men fight outside a pub. A barmaid sees them and is frightened. What offence?
Affray. Riot requires 12+ people; violent disorder requires 3+. Two persons fighting with a bystander (the barmaid) fearing for her safety = affray. (Answer: C)
Q4: A man holds noisy parties at his house over several days, disturbing neighbours. The neighbours ask the chief officer of police to stop it. What is the legal basis?
Causing harassment, alarm or distress (s 5 POA 1986). Breach of the peace requires violence, not noise. It's not a trespassory assembly or rave (the partygoers are not trespassers). Public assemblies don't need authorisation. (Answer: D)
Q5: Animal rights protesters gather on the pavement outside a shop. They are peaceful but the street is completely blocked. The police ask them to disperse; they refuse and are arrested. What offence?
Obstruction of the highway (s 137 Highways Act 1980). The normal use of the highway is for passage and repassage. When the street is completely blocked, the protest goes beyond reasonable use. (Answer: A)
Q6: What is the key difference between a public procession and a public assembly under the POA 1986?
A procession is a moving demonstration; an assembly is static. The practical difference: processions can be banned district-wide (with Home Secretary approval), while assemblies cannot be banned — only individual assemblies can be subject to conditions.
Q7: When can the police use "kettling" lawfully?
When the detention is reasonable and proportionate to prevent a breach of the peace, even if it restricts Art 5 ECHR (right to liberty). Confirmed in Austin v Commissioner of Police [2009].
Q8: Why were the speakers in Redmond-Bate v DPP [2000] not guilty of breach of the peace?
Their speech was lawful and protected by Art 10 ECHR (free expression). Although it attracted a hostile crowd, the speakers did not provoke violence. The police should have controlled the violent crowd members, not arrested the speakers.
Q9: What distinguishes violent disorder from riot?
Violent disorder requires only 3+ persons (not 12), does not require a common purpose, and is committed by those who use OR threaten violence (riot requires actual use of violence for a common purpose).
Q10: In R v Jones [2006], why did the defendants' defence of "lawful activity" fail?
They argued that preparing weapons for an aggressive war in Iraq was not lawful under international law. The House of Lords held that under UK law the activity was lawful — therefore the defendants had no defence to aggravated trespass.
