📝 Practice Questions

Q1: Under HRA s2, what must UK courts do regarding EctHR case law?
UK courts must "take into account" EctHR case law. They normally should follow it (Alconbury), but may depart where there is no EctHR decision, a conflict, or a misunderstanding of UK law. Lower UK courts must follow higher UK court precedents.
Q2: What is the difference between s3 and s4 of the HRA 1998?
s3 requires courts to read legislation compatibly with Convention rights "so far as possible" — this can include departing from literal meaning or "reading down." s4 allows courts to issue a Declaration of Incompatibility when s3 interpretation is impossible — but the law remains valid. s3 is about interpretation; s4 is about declaring a problem Parliament must fix.
Q3: Why does s4 of the HRA 1998 NOT violate Parliamentary Sovereignty?
Because a Declaration of Incompatibility does not invalidate the law — it merely flags the incompatibility. Parliament retains the choice of whether and how to amend the legislation. Sovereignty is preserved.
Q4: What is a "public authority" under s6 of the HRA 1998?
A public authority is any body that performs functions of a public nature. It is unlawful for a public authority to act in a way that is incompatible with a Convention right. Courts and tribunals are also public authorities under s6.
Q5: Who has standing to bring a claim under s7 of the HRA 1998?
Only a "victim" — a person who is personally affected by the alleged violation. Pressure groups and organisations cannot bring claims as victims (NIHRC v re abortion law [2015]). Claims are usually brought via judicial review with a 1-year time limit.
Q6: What are the four steps of the Proportionality Test?
(1) Is there a legitimate aim? (2) Would the restriction achieve that aim? (3) Is there a less intrusive restriction available? (4) Is there a fair balance between the right and the community need?
Q7: Why is Art 3 classified as an "absolute right"? What does this mean in practice?
Art 3 (Prohibition of Torture) is absolute — it admits NO exceptions and CANNOT be derogated from, even in times of war or public emergency. In practice, this means the UK cannot deport someone to a country where they face a real risk of torture (Chahal v UK [1996]), even if the person is a national security threat.
Q8: List the six lawful grounds for deprivation of liberty under Art 5(1).
(a) Detention after lawful conviction; (b) Non-compliance with a court order; (c) Lawful arrest on reasonable suspicion; (d) Detention of persons of unsound mind; (e) Detention of alcohol/drug addicts; (f) Prevention of unlawful migration.
Q9: What are the three essential elements of a fair trial under Art 6?
Independent + Impartial + Established by Law. The tribunal must be free from external pressure, have no preconceived position, and have a legal basis for its existence.
Q10: Explain the three-step analysis for Art 8 claims.
Step 1: Is there an interference with private/family life, home, or correspondence? Step 2: Is the interference lawful (prescribed by law)? Step 3: Is the interference necessary and proportionate in a democratic society? The burden of justification is on the state at Step 3.
Q11: What is the difference between the "internal forum" and "manifestation" under Art 9?
The internal forum (inner beliefs) is absolutely protected — the state cannot force you to change your beliefs. Manifestation (expressing religion publicly) is a qualified right — it can be limited if necessary for public safety, public order, or the protection of others' rights.
Q12: What is Habeas Corpus and which Article of the ECHR is it linked to?
Habeas Corpus (人身保护令) is an ancient writ allowing any unlawfully detained person to apply to the High Court to challenge the lawfulness of their detention. It is linked to Art 5(4) — the right to challenge detention. The court can order the detaining authority to bring the person before a judge.
