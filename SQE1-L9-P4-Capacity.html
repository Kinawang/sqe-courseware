📖 Part 4: Capacity

If a person lacks capacity, they may not be legally bound by an agreement.

Key term: capacity (缔约能力)
The ability of a person to enter into and be legally bound by an agreement.

1. Mental Incapacity (精神能力欠缺)

Governed by the Mental Capacity Act 2005. Two tests must be satisfied:

  1. The person did not have mental capacity at the time of entering the agreement
  2. The other party knew or should have known of the incapacity

Under s 3(1) MCA 2005, a person is unable to make a decision if they cannot:

  • Understand the information relevant to the decision
  • Retain that information
  • Use or weigh that information as part of the decision-making process
  • Communicate their decision
Key term: voidable (可撤销)
A voidable agreement is valid and binding, but can be set aside at the request of the party who has the remedy. A contract with a person lacking mental capacity is voidable.

Exception — Necessaries (必需品): Under s 7(2) MCA 2005, a person with mental incapacity must pay a reasonable price for goods and services they require at the time. "Necessaries" covers more than bare necessities — but if the person already has a good supply, the goods are unlikely to be necessaries.

2. Intoxication (醉酒)

An agreement may also be voidable for a person who is intoxicated and unable to understand the nature of their actions. The same two-test applies: (1) the person was intoxicated, (2) the other party knew or ought to have known. The necessaries rule also applies.

3. Contracts with Minors (未成年人)

A minor is a person under 18 years old. Governed by the Minors' Contracts Act 1987.

  • Agreements with minors are voidable
  • Minors must pay a reasonable amount for necessaries
  • Minors ARE bound by agreements for employment, education and training — unless they lose more than they gain
  • The court may order a minor to return goods if it is just and equitable to do so
  • If a person with capacity (e.g., a parent) guarantees the minor's obligations, the guarantor is bound

Practice Example 3.3 (Nash v Inman [1908])

Joe, a minor, buys 11 fancy waistcoats from Vladimir, who knows Joe is a minor. Joe already has plenty of suitable clothes.

Answer: Joe is not bound to pay. The waistcoats are not necessaries because Joe already had plenty of suitable clothes. There was no evidence that he required them at the time.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. A wealthy woman and her fiancé sign a prenuptial agreement providing that her assets remain hers after marriage. They later divorce. Can the man claim the woman's assets?
Answer: No — Prenuptial agreements are enforceable despite being in the social and domestic context. The seriousness of the subject matter (wealthy parties, substantial assets) rebuts the presumption of no intention to create legal relations. The man cannot claim the woman's assets covered by the agreement.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. A wealthy woman and her fiancé sign a prenuptial agreement providing that her assets remain hers after marriage. They later divorce. Can the man claim the woman's assets?
Answer: No — Prenuptial agreements are enforceable despite being in the social and domestic context. The seriousness of the subject matter (wealthy parties, substantial assets) rebuts the presumption of no intention to create legal relations. The man cannot claim the woman's assets covered by the agreement.
Q2. A mother promises her daughter that if she gives up her job in New York and studies law in London, the mother will pay fees, provide an allowance, and provide a house. The daughter relies on this and moves to London. After five years, the mother seeks to reclaim the house. Is the mother bound?
Answer: No — Although detrimental reliance and seriousness are rebutting factors, the lack of certainty as to how long the arrangement should last undermines these factors. The mother and daughter were on friendly terms, and the presumption of no intention is not rebutted. The mother is not bound.
Q3. An insurance company advertises holiday insurance for anyone booking through a network of holiday companies. A man books a holiday and his holiday is cancelled due to a pandemic. Can he claim against the insurance company?
Answer: Yes — The advertisement is not a gimmick but a binding offer to provide protection to anyone who books a holiday with a member of the network. The man accepted the offer by booking. The commercial presumption of intention applies and is not rebutted.
Q4. A businesswoman sells her zoo to a businessman. They sign a separate document allowing her to visit the animals "at times and subject to conditions to be agreed." What rights does she acquire?
Answer: None — The separate document is an agreement to agree. It contains no essential terms (times and conditions are left for future agreement). Agreements to agree are not enforceable. The businesswoman acquires no rights under the separate document.
Q5. A 16-year-old schoolgirl buys a diamond tiara and a pearl necklace but fails to pay. What recourse does the shop owner have?
Answer: Court order for return — The tiara and necklace are not necessaries for a 16-year-old schoolgirl. The contract is voidable (not void — that would be if contracts with minors were void, but they are voidable). The shop owner can petition the court to order the schoolgirl to return the goods if it is just and equitable to do so.
Q6. Two friends agree to meet at a restaurant for dinner. One fails to show up, causing the other to wait for an hour. Can the waiting friend sue for breach of contract?
Answer: No — This is in the social and domestic context. The presumption is no intention to create legal relations. There is no evidence to rebut the presumption (the agreement is not serious in a legal sense, the terms are not certain, and there is no detrimental reliance).
Q7. A commercial contract contains the clause "Binding in honour only." Is the agreement legally binding?
Answer: No — Despite being in the commercial context (where the presumption is intention to create legal relations), the clear words "Binding in honour only" rebut the presumption. The parties have expressly stated they do not intend the agreement to have legal effect.
Q8. A parent company writes a comfort letter to a bank stating its policy is that its subsidiary will always be in a position to meet its liabilities. The subsidiary collapses. Can the bank claim against the parent?
Answer: No — Following Kleinwort Benson v Malaysia Mining Corp, the statement is merely a description of current policy at the time of signing, not a promise as to future conduct. The comfort letter does not create legal relations. The fact the parent refused to provide a guarantee supports this interpretation.
Q9. An agreement to sell goods states the price will be "determined by a mutually agreed valuer." Is this agreement void for uncertainty?
Answer: No — Although the price is not specified, the agreement provides a mechanism for determining the missing term (appointment of a valuer). Agreements that provide for third-party determination of key terms are not void for lack of certainty.
Q10. A person suffering from schizophrenia enters into a contract to buy a car. The seller is unaware of the buyer's condition. Can the buyer set aside the contract?
Answer: No — Although the buyer may lack mental capacity (test 1), the second test is not satisfied: the seller did not know and should not have known of the buyer's incapacity. Both tests must be satisfied for the contract to be voidable.
Q11. A 17-year-old enters into an employment contract as an apprentice. The terms are favourable overall but he loses on one particular clause. Can he avoid the contract?
Answer: No — Minors ARE bound by contracts of employment, education and training, unless they lose more than they gain overall. The fact that one clause is unfavourable does not make the contract voidable if the minor gains more than they lose overall.
Q12. True or false? A company's constitutional documents restrict it from entering into property transactions. The company nevertheless signs a lease with a landlord acting in good faith. Is the lease binding?
Answer: True — the lease is binding. A party entering into an agreement with a company in good faith will be bound, regardless of any restriction in the company's constitutional documents. The company cannot use its internal restrictions to escape contractual obligations.
