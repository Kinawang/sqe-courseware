📖 Part 1: The EU's Law-Making Powers & Direct Effect

Why EU Law Still Matters after Brexit

Two reasons why you still need to know EU law for SQE1:

  1. Retained EU law: EU law that applied in the UK before "exit day" (31 Jan 2020) remains in force as retained EU law (a new category of domestic UK law).
  2. The Withdrawal Agreement (TCA): The TCA between the UK and EU is directly enforceable in UK courts and must be interpreted in accordance with EU law principles.

The Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU)

The CJEU (formerly ECJ) interprets EU law and ensures uniform application across all member states. Before Brexit, any UK court or tribunal could refer a question of EU law to the CJEU. The Supreme Court was obliged to make a reference if a case involved EU law. The EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 ended this referral obligation.

Direct Effect (直接效力)

The CJEU devised the principle of direct effect to ensure EU law is obeyed and interpreted the same way in every member state.

Direct Effect: Claimants can enforce rights created by EU law in national courts (without needing to go to the CJEU). There are two types:
  • Vertical direct effect: The EU law may be enforced against a state authority (a "public body", "an emanation of the state").
  • Horizontal direct effect: The EU law may also be enforced against private persons and organisations.

The Different Types of EU Law

TypeDirect Effect?Vertical/Horizontal?Implementation Required?
Regulations (法规)✅ YES (if wording clear, precise, unconditional)✅ Both vertical AND horizontal❌ NO — directly applicable in all member states
Decisions (决定)✅ YES (against the addressee)✅ Both vertical AND horizontal❌ NO — binding on the member state to which addressed
Articles of EU Treaties (条约条款)✅ YES (if clear, unconditional, precise — Van Gend en Loos [1963])✅ Both vertical AND horizontal❌ NO — directly enforceable; UK estimated 48 TFEU articles had direct effect at exit
Directives (指令)⚠️ Vertical ONLY (against public bodies); indirect effect for private parties✅ Vertical ONLY (NO horizontal effect)⚠️ YES — member states must implement by specified date, but choose how
Van Gend en Loos v Nederlandse Administratie der Belastingen [1963] ECR 1
Principle: Articles in the EU Treaty can have direct effect. If the wording is clear, unconditional, and precise, the article creates an individual right enforceable in national courts — even if it contradicts national law. Member states have "limited their sovereign rights" by joining the EU.

Directives and Vertical Direct Effect

Directives cannot be enforced against a non-state, private defendant. To enforce directives against private parties, the CJEU developed:

  • Indirect effect: Even if a directive does not have direct effect, the national court is required to interpret national law to give effect to the directive.
  • The Francovich principle (损害赔偿): In Francovich v Italian State [1992], the CJEU ordered a state to pay damages to individuals injured by its failure to implement a directive. This has been extended to serious breaches of other types of EU law (regulations, articles) that injure individuals.
Francovich v Italian State [1992] IRLR 84
Facts: Italy failed to implement an EU directive on employee protection in insolvency. Employees lost money.
Held: A state must pay damages to individuals for injury caused by failure to implement a directive. This is now a general principle: if a state commits a serious breach of EU law causing damage to an individual, the state must compensate.
⚠️ SQE1 note: The EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 s4 states that the right to damages under Francovich ceased to apply on or after IP completion day.

Supremacy of EU Law (欧盟法至上性)

Costa v ENEL [1964] ECR 585
Principle: Member states have made "partial transfers of sovereignty" to the EU and therefore cannot make laws incompatible with EU law. EU law has supremacy over national law. National courts must set aside conflicting national laws.
Significance: This is the foundation of the conflict between EU law and parliamentary sovereignty in the UK (resolved by Factortame).

📖 Part 2: EU Law and UK Law (ECA 1972)

The European Communities Act 1972 (ECA 1972)

When the UK joined the EU in 1973, the ECA 1972 gave effect to EU law in UK law. Its main provisions:

SectionEffect
s2(1)Made existing EU law part of UK law; permitted the EU to legislate for the UK in the future (no need for new UK legislation to implement new EU laws).
s2(2)Delegated legislation could be used to make EU law (particularly new directives) part of UK law. Also allowed delegated legislation to repeal or amend existing Acts of Parliament (to implement EU law).
s2(4)Any enactment (past or future) "shall be construed and have effect subject to" the ECA 1972 — i.e., UK courts must interpret UK law to comply with EU law.
s3Instructed UK courts to enforce EU law and decide cases "in accordance with the principles laid down by... the European Court".

Key Case: Factortame (EU Law Supremacy in the UK)

R v Secretary of State for Transport, ex parte Factortame (No 2) [1991] AC 603
Facts: Spanish fisherman challenged UK legislation that restricted fishing rights. The UK law explicitly contradicted directly effective articles of the TFEU. UK courts could not interpret the UK law to make it compatible with EU law.
Held (House of Lords):
  1. The ECA 1972 voluntarily gave permission to the EU to legislate for the UK.
  2. The Act also made clear that EU law had supremacy over UK Acts of Parliament.
  3. A UK court must "override any rule of national law found to be in conflict with any directly enforceable rule of community law".
Significance: This was a constitutional revolution: parliamentary sovereignty was temporarily limited by the ECA 1972. EU law could override UK primary legislation.
⚠️ SQE1 Exam Warning: The ECA 1972 has been repealed with Brexit. BUT you still need to know about it because: (1) retained EU law derives from it; (2) the Withdrawal Agreement Act 2020 contains similar provisions for the TCA (temporary limitation on sovereignty again).

📖 Part 3: Withdrawal from the EU

The Withdrawal Process (Art 50 TEU)

Article 50 of the Treaty on European Union (TEU) sets out the procedure for a member state to withdraw:

  • A member state must give notice of withdrawal to the EU "in accordance with its own constitutional requirements".
  • The UK government considered that joining/leaving treaties was a matter of the royal prerogative and wished to notify the EU without Parliament's consent.
R (Miller) v Secretary of State for Exiting the EU [2017] UKSC 5
Facts: The government argued it could trigger Art 50 using prerogative power. Gina Miller (a concerned citizen) challenged this.
Held (Supreme Court): Because leaving the EU would change UK domestic law by altering the constitutional position and changing rights of residents, an Act of Parliament was required (see Lesson 4 on prerogative).
Result: The EU (Notification of Withdrawal) Act 2017 was passed to authorise notification.

Key Dates

TermDateMeaning
Exit Day (脱欧日)31 January 2020The day the UK ceased to be a member of the EU.
IP Completion Day (过渡期结束日)31 December 2020The day the implementation period ended; EU law ceased to have effect in the UK. Also called "implementation period completion day".
Exam Tip: Remember the difference! Exit Day = 31 Jan 2020 (UK left EU); IP Completion Day = 31 Dec 2020 (transition period ended, EU law stopped applying). SQE1 questions may test this distinction.

📖 Part 4: The EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 and Retained EU Law

The 2018 Act repeals the ECA 1972 but converts existing EU law into domestic law (called "retained EU law") to avoid a legal vacuum.

Section-by-Section Analysis

s1: Repeal of ECA 1972

The ECA 1972 is repealed on exit day, but continued to have effect until IP completion day. Parliament has withdrawn its permission for the EU to make laws for the UK. No new EU laws will be accepted.

s2: EU-Derived Domestic Legislation Retained

All delegated legislation made under s2(2) ECA 1972 to implement EU law remains in force. Also, any other UK legislation enacted to implement EU law is retained.

s3: Direct EU Legislation Retained ("Retained EU Law")

Direct EU legislation — meaning EU regulations, EU decisions directed at the UK, and EU tertiary legislation — which were in force immediately before exit day, become part of domestic law as retained EU law.

Retained EU Law (保留欧盟法): EU law that was in force in the UK before "exit day" (31 Jan 2020) remains in force. It is no longer EU law — it is converted into domestic UK law by the EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018.

s4: Rights and Obligations Retained

Any rights, powers, liabilities, obligations, restrictions, remedies and procedures that were available under the ECA 1972 before exit day are also retained. This covers articles and directives that had been held by courts to have direct effect before exit day.

⚠️ Warning: s4 also states that the right to damages under the Francovich principle ceased to apply on or after IP completion day.

s5: The Supremacy of EU Law ENDS (for new laws)

The principle of the supremacy of EU law does not apply to any enactment passed after exit day.

However: As existing EU law is retained, the supremacy principle continues to apply where retained EU law is incompatible with domestic law made before exit day. If retained EU law conflicts with UK law made before exit day, the retained EU law must be applied in preference.

ScenarioWhich law wins?
Retained EU law vs UK law made BEFORE exit day✅ Retained EU law wins (supremacy preserved)
Retained EU law vs UK law made AFTER exit day✅ UK law wins (supremacy of EU law does NOT apply to post-exit-day UK law)

s6: Court Interpretation of Retained EU Law

  • UK courts cannot refer cases to the CJEU on or after IP completion day.
  • UK courts are not bound by CJEU decisions made on or after IP completion day.
  • UK courts must decide the meaning of retained EU law "in accordance with any retained case law and retained general principles of EU law".
  • UK courts may consider (but are not bound by) CJEU decisions made after IP completion day to help them decide.
  • The Supreme Court is NOT bound by any retained EU case law and may depart from it (using the 1966 Practice Statement test — same as for domestic law).
  • The Court of Appeal is also NOT bound by retained EU case law.
  • Lower courts and tribunals ARE bound by retained EU case law.

Delegated Legislation Powers (s8 ff)

The 2018 Act grants extensive power to ministers to make delegated legislation (for 2 years after IP completion day) to deal with problems caused by retained EU law. This secondary legislation may amend or repeal Acts of Parliament (a rare power for delegated legislation).

The devolved legislatures (Scotland, Wales, NI) also have these powers, but only within their devolved competence. The UK Parliament can override the devolved legislatures. The UK Internal Markets Act 2020 grants similar powers to UK government ministers to prevent devolved institutions replacing retained EU law with laws inconsistent with UK government wishes.

📖 Part 5: The Withdrawal Agreement (TCA) and EU(Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020

The Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA)

The UK finalised its Withdrawal Agreement with the EU in late 2020. The TCA contains provisions on:

  • Trade
  • Protection of rights of EU citizens resident in the UK, and UK citizens resident in the EU
  • The Northern Ireland Protocol (aims to keep the border between ROI and NI open)

The TCA is Enforceable in UK Law

EU (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020: Gives the TCA legal effect in UK law. Adds s7A to the EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018. This gives existing and future rights under the TCA legal effect in the UK and allows them to be enforced in UK courts.

Key provisions:

  • Article 4: The UK agreed to use EU law to decide the meaning of the Withdrawal Agreement and apply it in preference to UK law. Incompatible UK laws must be disapplied.
  • The agreement can be enforced in UK courts; rights granted can have direct effect in UK courts.
  • CJEU cases decided before the end of the transition period must be followed; UK courts must have regard to CJEU cases decided after the end of that period.
  • UK law must be interpreted to be compatible with the Withdrawal Agreement.
Practice Example 9.2 (from textbook):
Facts: A client has a contract to ship goods to France. A UK Act of Parliament seems to contradict an article in the TCA.
Advice: The 2020 Acts require that, if possible, the UK Act must be interpreted to be compatible with the TCA. If that is not possible, the issue of parliamentary sovereignty arises. The (Withdrawal Agreement) Act suggests that the TCA article would have supremacy — unless the UK Act was passed after 2020 and is expressly worded to contradict the TCA.

Parliamentary Sovereignty Reaffirmed

s38 of the EU (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020 reaffirm that "the Parliament of the UK is sovereign" and "nothing in this Act derogates from" this.

This means: the UK Parliament is allowing the EU to "legislate" (via the TCA) for limited purposes, but can, if it wishes, withdraw that permission with a later Act of Parliament.

⚠️ Exam Warning: There is still uncertainty about how the TCA will be interpreted. UK courts must decide which articles of the TCA have direct effect, and whether, if they conflict with UK law, they override Acts of Parliament. Watch for first cases on this.

📋 Chapter Summary: EU Law & Retained EU Law

  1. EU law-making: Regulations (direct effect, vertical+horizontal); Directives (vertical direct effect only); Articles (direct effect if clear/precise/unconditional); Francovich (state liability for non-implementation).
  2. ECA 1972: Gave effect to EU law in UK; Factortame held EU law had supremacy over UK Acts of Parliament.
  3. Withdrawal: Art 50 TEU; Miller [2017] (Parliament must authorize); Exit Day 31 Jan 2020; IP Completion Day 31 Dec 2020.
  4. EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018: Repeals ECA 1972; converts EU law into "retained EU law"; supremacy preserved for retained EU law vs pre-exit-day UK law ONLY; no more CJEU references; Supreme Court NOT bound by retained EU case law.
  5. TCA / 2020 Act: Withdrawal Agreement enforceable in UK courts; UK law must be interpreted compatibly; parliamentary sovereignty reaffirmed (s38).

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: What is the difference between "exit day" and "IP completion day"?
Exit day = 31 Jan 2020: The UK ceased to be a member of the EU. IP completion day = 31 Dec 2020: The implementation period ended; EU law ceased to have effect in the UK. EU law made before 31 Jan 2020 becomes retained EU law; EU law made between 31 Jan and 31 Dec 2020 also remained in force until IP completion day.
Q2: Can a directive be enforced against a private employer? Which case established this?
NO. Directives have vertical direct effect ONLY — they can only be enforced against a public body ("an emanation of the state"). They cannot be enforced against a private employer. However, the court must use indirect effect — interpret domestic law to give effect to the directive. (This is the fact pattern of an SQE1-style question.)
Q3: What did the EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 s5 provide about the supremacy of EU law?
s5 states that the principle of the supremacy of EU law does not apply to any enactment passed after exit day. However, for existing law (retained EU law), the supremacy principle continues where retained EU law conflicts with UK law made before exit day. Retained EU law wins over pre-exit-day UK law; post-exit-day UK law wins over retained EU law.
Q4: Can UK courts still refer questions to the CJEU after Brexit? Are they bound by post-Brexit CJEU decisions?
NO. Under s6 of the 2018 Act, UK courts cannot refer cases to the CJEU, and are not bound by CJEU decisions made on or after IP completion day. However, they "may have regard to" (may consider) post-Brexit CJEU decisions to help interpret retained EU law. The Supreme Court is NOT bound by any retained EU case law (can depart using 1966 Practice Statement).
Q5: A client's equal pay claim relies on an EU directive that gives her greater rights than UK legislation. Can she rely on it after Brexit?
The directive only applies in the UK (as retained EU law) if the CJEU decided it had direct effect BEFORE exit day. If it did, it has supremacy over UK legislation if that legislation was also enacted before exit day. Also warn her: directives only have vertical direct effect — she can only enforce it if her employer is a state employer, NOT a private employer.
Q6: What is the Francovich principle and does it still apply after Brexit?
Francovich [1992]: A state must pay damages to individuals injured by its failure to implement a directive (or serious breach of other EU law). After Brexit: s4 of the EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 states that the right to damages under Francovich ceased to apply on or after IP completion day (31 Dec 2020). It is no longer available for new claims.
Q7: What did the Supreme Court hold in Miller [2017] and why was it constitutionally significant?
The Supreme Court held that the government could NOT use prerogative power to trigger Art 50 TEU (giving notice of withdrawal from the EU). Because leaving the EU would change UK domestic law and alter rights of residents, an Act of Parliament was required. This reaffirmed that prerogative cannot be used to change domestic law (consistent with Lesson 4 on prerogative limitations).
Q8: Under the Withdrawal Agreement (TCA), how must UK courts interpret UK law? Does the TCA have supremacy over UK Acts of Parliament?
Under the 2020 Act, every enactment (past and future) must be read and given effect "subject to" the TCA requirements — i.e., UK courts must interpret UK law to be compatible with the TCA. If interpretation is not possible, the TCA would have supremacy over UK law — unless the UK Act was passed after 2020 and is expressly worded to contradict the TCA. (This is still untested in courts.)
Q9: Which courts are bound by "retained EU case law" and which are not?
NOT bound: Supreme Court (can depart under 1966 Practice Statement); Court of Appeal (can also depart). Bound: Lower courts and tribunals MUST follow retained EU case law. This is the same hierarchy as for domestic case law — retained EU case law is now just another category of domestic precedent.
Q10: Can delegated legislation be used to amend or repeal Acts of Parliament under the EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018?
YES — but only for a limited time (2 years after IP completion day). The 2018 Act grants ministers extensive power to make delegated legislation to deal with problems caused by retained EU law. This delegated legislation may amend or repeal Acts of Parliament — a rare and significant power. The devolved legislatures have similar powers within their competence.
Q11: Does the EU (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020 permanently limit parliamentary sovereignty?
NO. s38 reaffirms that "the Parliament of the UK is sovereign". The UK Parliament is allowing the EU to "legislate" for the UK for the limited purposes of the TCA, but can withdraw that permission with a later Act. This is the same temporary limitation mechanism as the ECA 1972 — Parliament can always repeal.
Q12: A case in a UK court involves a question about the interpretation of retained EU law. There is a relevant CJEU decision from 2023. What should the court do?
Under s6 of the 2018 Act, the UK court is NOT bound by the 2023 CJEU decision. However, it "may have regard to" (may consider) that decision to help it decide the meaning of retained EU law. The court must also follow any retained EU case law (CJEU decisions made before IP completion day). If it is the Supreme Court or Court of Appeal, it may depart from retained EU case law using the 1966 Practice Statement test.
