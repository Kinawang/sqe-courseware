📖 Part 1: Doctrine of Privity of Contract

Privity of contract (合同相对性) is an English law doctrine establishing the general rule that a person who is not a party to a contract (a third party) cannot acquire any rights under that contract or be subject to its burdens.

Key term: privity of contract (合同相对性)
The English law doctrine that a third party cannot acquire rights under a contract or be subject to its burdens.
Key term: third party (第三方)
In relation to a contract, a third party is a person who is not a party to the contract.

Practice Example 4.1 (Dunlop v Selfridge [1915])

Dennis sells tyres to David with a condition that David must not sell below a fixed price, and must require any subsequent buyer to agree the same. David sells to Selina, who agrees not to sell below the fixed price. Selina sells below the fixed price.

Answer: Dennis cannot force Selina not to sell below the fixed price. Selina is a third party to the contract between Dennis and David. The doctrine of privity means only a party to a contract can bring a claim under it.

The rule that only a party to a contract can bring a claim remains the general rule, unless a recognised exception applies. Exceptions are found under statute and at common law.

📖 Part 2: Contracts (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999

The Contracts (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999 (CRTPA) was enacted to allow a third party to acquire rights under a contract to which it is not a party.

Under CRTPA 1999, a third party can:

  • Enforce remedies for breach of contract
  • Rely on a clause that limits or excludes their liability
Important: A third party is NOT required to provide consideration to acquire rights under CRTPA 1999.

Enforceability Criteria

For a third party to acquire rights under CRTPA 1999, ONE of the following must be satisfied:

  1. The contract must expressly state that the third party may enforce a term — either by referring to the third party by name, or as a class of person (e.g., "any previous tenant"); OR
  2. A term in the contract must purport to confer a benefit on the third party, AND no other provisions show that the parties did not intend the term to be enforceable by the third party.

Practice Example 4.2

Graham assigns his shop lease to Margaret and guarantees her rent to PropertyCo. Margaret's separate agreement with PropertyCo limits liability to £5,000 for "any previous tenant" on a guarantee. Margaret fails to pay £10,000 rent.

Answer: PropertyCo's claim against Graham is limited to £5,000. Graham falls within the description of "any previous tenant" — the CRTPA 1999 applies, giving Graham the right to rely on the limitation clause despite being a third party to the Margaret-PropertyCo agreement.

Excluding CRTPA 1999

The parties to a contract may exclude CRTPA 1999 by clearly stating in the agreement that the Act does not apply. This prevents third parties from acquiring rights under the contract.

Exam warning: Always check whether CRTPA 1999 has been excluded. If it has, the third party cannot rely on statutory rights and must look to common law exceptions instead.

📖 Part 3: Common Law Exceptions to Privity

Since CRTPA 1999 does not apply in all scenarios, you must know the common law structures that allow third parties to acquire rights.

1. Assignment (权利转让)

A person who has rights under an agreement may transfer (assign) those rights to a third party. To be effective, an assignment must be:

  • Permitted under the terms of the relevant agreement
  • Absolute (final)
  • In writing

Notice of the assignment must be given to the person against whom the third party acquires rights.

2. Agency (代理)

Where an agreement is made between Party A and Party B, but Party A is acting as Party C's agent, the legal position is that Party A has made an agreement between Party C and Party B. Party C is a direct party (not a third party) and is entitled to rights and remedies.

Requirements for the agency structure:

  • It must be clear in the main agreement that the third party is intended to be protected by the limitation clause
  • It must be clear that the party to the main contract is acting as agent for the third party
  • The party must have authority from the third party to act as agent
  • The third party must provide consideration to the promisor

Practice Example 4.3

Tallships contracts with Deliverit to transport cargo. Deliverit separately contracts with Dockhand to unload. The Tallships-Deliverit agreement limits Deliverit's liability to £1,000 and extends this to Dockhand. Deliverit acts as agent for Dockhand. Dockhand damages the cargo (not negligently). Cost: £20,000.

Answer: Dockhand is protected by the limitation clause. All agency requirements are satisfied: the agreement clearly intends to protect Dockhand, Deliverit acts as agent with authority, and Dockhand provides consideration by unloading the cargo.

3. Collateral Contracts (附带合同)

A collateral contract is a separate contract (in addition to the main contract) identified between the promisor and the third party. It gives the third party direct contractual rights against the promisor.

Practice Example 4.4 (Shanklin Pier v Detel Products [1951])

Coastal owns a pier and hires Brushco to paint it. Coastal instructs Brushco to use Chemco's paint because it is advertised as guaranteed to last ten years. Brushco buys the paint. After one year, the paint washes off.

Answer: Coastal can claim against Chemco under a collateral contract. Chemco's advertisement is an offer; Coastal accepts by instructing Brushco to buy the paint; Coastal's consideration is the instruction to Brushco. Coastal has direct contractual rights against Chemco despite being a third party to the Brushco-Chemco contract.

4. Trusts (信托)

A party to a contract may declare that they hold rights under the contract on trust for a third party (the beneficiary). If a valid trust is created, the beneficiary can enforce the rights through the trustee. However, a formal declaration of trust is required, and the promisor must be aware of it.

Exam warning: For SQE1, when a third party cannot claim in their own name, check all four common law structures: (1) Assignment — was the rights transfer valid? (2) Agency — was the party acting as agent with authority? (3) Collateral contract — is there a separate contract between the third party and the promisor? (4) Trust — was there a declaration of trust?

📖 Part 4: Remedies the Promisee Can Exercise for a Third Party

Where a third party cannot bring a claim in their own name, they must rely on the promisee (the party to the contract) to bring a claim on their behalf.

Damages

General rule: A party to a contract cannot sue and recover for the loss of a third party. However, there are exceptions:

Exception 1: Contracts of Convenience (便利合同)

One person may enter into a contract on their own behalf and on behalf of others for reasons of convenience. Key examples:

  • Family holidays
  • Ordering meals in restaurants for a party
  • Hiring a taxi for a group

Practice Example 4.6 (Jackson v Horizon Holidays [1975])

James books a holiday for himself, his wife, and two children for £1,400. The hotel is unsatisfactory — the whole family suffers discomfort and distress.

Answer: James can recover damages for himself and his family members. He contracted on behalf of himself and his family. This is a contract of convenience — a limited exception to the rule that a promisee can only recover for their own loss.

Exception 2: The St Martins Property Exception

In the commercial context, a promisee can sue for a third party's loss even if the promisee has suffered no loss. Requirements:

  • It was in the contemplation of both parties that the property would be transferred to another party
  • It was the intention of the parties that the promisee is treated as entering into the contract for the benefit of those who may acquire an interest in the subject matter

Practice Example 4.7

LawCo hires SolutionsCo to remove asbestos from its leased building. LawCo tells SolutionsCo it plans to sell the lease. SolutionsCo completes the work. LawCo assigns the lease to OtherCo, who finds asbestos still present.

Answer: LawCo can sue SolutionsCo for OtherCo's loss, even though LawCo has suffered no loss itself. Both parties knew the building would be transferred, and the contract was intended to benefit future owners. This is the St Martins Property exception.

Specific Performance (实际履行)

A discretionary remedy available where damages would not be adequate. The promisee must be willing to enforce their rights. Only appropriate if the promisor has not yet fully performed their obligations.

Injunctions (禁令)

A promisee may obtain an injunction to prevent a promisor from doing something they agreed not to do.

Exam warning: When a third party cannot claim in their own name, check: (1) Is this a contract of convenience (family holiday, restaurant meal, taxi)? (2) Does the St Martins Property exception apply (commercial context, property transfer contemplated)? If yes, the promisee can recover for the third party's loss.

📝 Key Point Checklist

  • Privity of contract: third parties cannot acquire rights or be subject to burdens under a contract
  • CRTPA 1999: statutory exception — third party can enforce terms if expressly named/identified OR if a term purports to confer a benefit (unless excluded)
  • CRTPA 1999: no consideration required from the third party
  • CRTPA 1999 can be excluded by clear statement in the contract
  • Assignment: transfer of rights — must be permitted, absolute, in writing, with notice
  • Agency: party acts as agent for third party — requires authority, consideration from third party
  • Collateral contract: separate contract between promisor and third party (e.g., Shanklin Pier)
  • Trust: promisee holds rights on trust for third party — requires declaration of trust
  • Contracts of convenience: promisee can recover for third parties (family holidays, meals, taxis)
  • St Martins Property exception: promisee can recover for third party's loss even with no own loss (commercial, property transfer)
  • Specific performance and injunctions: available via promisee at court's discretion

📝 Practice Questions

Q1. A manufacturer sells chairs to a dealer with a price-fixing condition. The dealer sells to a discounter who agrees not to sell below the fixed price. The discounter sells below the fixed price. Can the manufacturer enforce the price-fixing term against the discounter?
Answer: No — The CRTPA 1999 enforceability criteria are not satisfied. There is no express statement in the contract that the discounter may enforce a term, and no term purports to confer a benefit on the manufacturer. There is no evidence of agency or a collateral contract. The doctrine of privity prevents the manufacturer from claiming against the discounter.
Q2. A woman sold her business to a company, which agreed to "complete outstanding orders taking into account any deposits paid by customers" and "pay any liabilities properly incurred." A customer with a defective bathroom claims against the company under CRTPA 1999. Can he?
Answer: No — The enforceability criteria are not satisfied. The customer is not expressly identified by name or as a class of persons. The term "liabilities" encompasses unidentified classes of third parties and does not satisfy the CRTPA 1999 requirements.
Q3. A developer instructs a decorating company to buy paint from a manufacturer whose advertisement promises the paint will last ten years. The contract between decorator and manufacturer excludes CRTPA 1999. The paint washes off after one week. Can the developer claim against the manufacturer?
Answer: Yes — Under a collateral contract. The manufacturer's advertisement is a unilateral offer; the developer accepts by instructing the decorator to buy the paint; the developer's consideration is the instruction to the decorator. The exclusion of CRTPA 1999 in the decorator-manufacturer contract does not affect the separate collateral contract.
Q4. A mother books a restaurant meal for herself, her husband, and six children. The food is inedible and everyone gets food poisoning. Can the mother claim for her family's losses?
Answer: Yes — This is a contract of convenience. The mother contracted on behalf of herself and her family. She can recover damages for her own loss and for the loss of those on whose behalf she made the contract.
Q5. A developer hires a cleaning company to decontaminate land, excluding CRTPA 1999. The developer tells the cleaning company she plans to sell the land. The cleaning company does defective work. The developer sells to a builder who finds contamination. Can the builder claim?
Answer: Yes, through the developer — The St Martins Property exception applies. The developer can sue the cleaning company for the builder's loss even though the developer has suffered no loss herself. Both parties contemplated the property transfer, and the contract was intended to benefit future owners. CRTPA 1999 exclusion is irrelevant because this is a common law exception.
Q6. A assigns his contractual right to receive £10,000 to C. The contract permits assignment. A gives notice to B (the promisor). Is the assignment effective?
Answer: Yes — All three requirements are satisfied: (1) the contract permits assignment, (2) the assignment is absolute (final transfer of the right), (3) it is in writing. Notice has been given to B. C can now enforce the right against B.
Q7. A shipping company's contract with a freight forwarder limits liability to £1,000 and states this applies to "any sub-contractor acting on behalf of the freight forwarder." The freight forwarder acts as agent for a sub-contractor who damages cargo. Can the sub-contractor rely on the limitation?
Answer: Yes — The agency structure applies. The agreement clearly intends to protect sub-contractors, the freight forwarder acts as agent with the sub-contractor's authority, and the sub-contractor provides consideration by performing the unloading work.
Q8. A contract states "This agreement does not confer any rights on third parties under the Contracts (Rights of Third Parties) Act 1999." A third party tries to enforce a term. Will they succeed?
Answer: No — The CRTPA 1999 has been expressly excluded by clear statement in the contract. The third party cannot rely on statutory rights and must look to common law exceptions (assignment, agency, collateral contract, or trust).
Q9. A landlord leases a shop to a tenant. The lease contains a clause limiting the liability of "any previous tenant" under a guarantee to £5,000. The tenant assigns the lease and guarantees the new tenant's obligations. The new tenant defaults on £10,000 rent. Can the landlord claim the full £10,000 from the guarantor?
Answer: No — The guarantor falls within the description of "any previous tenant" in the lease. Under CRTPA 1999, the guarantor can rely on the limitation clause capping liability at £5,000. The landlord's claim is limited to £5,000.
Q10. A person declares that they hold their contractual rights "on trust for" their sibling. The promisor is not aware of this declaration. Can the sibling enforce the rights?
Answer: No — A valid trust requires a declaration of trust, and the promisor must be aware of it. Since the promisor is not aware of the declaration, the trust is not effective. The sibling cannot enforce the rights through the trust structure.
Q11. A tenant hires a contractor to repair a building. Both know the tenant plans to assign the lease to a new tenant. The contractor does defective work. After the assignment, the new tenant discovers the defects. The original tenant has suffered no loss. Can the original tenant sue for the new tenant's loss?
Answer: Yes — The St Martins Property exception applies. Both parties contemplated the property transfer, and the contract was intended to benefit future owners. The original tenant can sue the contractor for the new tenant's loss even without having suffered any loss.
Q12. True or false? A third party relying on CRTPA 1999 must provide consideration to the promisor.
Answer: False — Unlike at common law (where agency requires consideration from the third party), CRTPA 1999 does not require the third party to provide any consideration to acquire rights under the contract.
