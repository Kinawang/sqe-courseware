📖 Part 1: What is Judicial Review?

Core Definition

Judicial Review (JR) (司法审查) is a procedure by which the High Court reviews the lawfulness of a decision, action, or failure to act by a public body.

⚠️ Exam Warning: JR is NOT an appeal. The court does NOT look at the merits of the decision (whether the judge agrees with it). JR only checks whether the decision was made legally — i.e., whether the decision-maker had the legal power to make that decision and followed the correct procedure.

JR vs Appeal — key distinction:

  • Appeal: The court reconsiders the merits and can substitute its own decision.
  • Judicial Review: The court checks legality only. If the decision is ultra vires (beyond powers), the court may quash it and remit it to the decision-maker to decide again — this time lawfully.

📖 Part 2: Application for Judicial Review

Step 1: Leave (Permission to Proceed)

Unlike ordinary civil claims, the claimant must first obtain permission to apply for JR. This is usually decided on the papers by a court official.

Permission GRANTED if:Permission REFUSED if:
✅ The claim is arguable and has a reasonable prospect of success❌ There is a suitable alternative remedy (e.g., a statutory appeal)
✅ The claim form is filed within 3 months of the grounds arising (6 weeks for planning decisions)❌ The outcome would not have been substantially different even if the complaint is upheld
❌ The claimant has delayed excessively
Practice Example: A client convicted in the magistrates' court wants to seek JR of his conviction. Advice: He has statutory rights of appeal (rehearing in Crown Court, or case stated to High Court). JR will almost certainly be refused — he must use the statutory appeal route first.

Step 2: Sufficient Interest (Locus Standi)

The claimant must have "sufficient interest" in the matter — meaning the decision affected some right or interest of the claimant.

CaseResultPrinciple
Schmidt v Secretary of State [1969]✅ Sufficient interest foundStudents expelled from UK — affected their rights
R v Liverpool Corporation [1972]✅ Taxi operators' association had sufficient interestThe decision affected their livelihood
Inland Revenue Commissioners v NFSE [1982]❌ NO sufficient interestTax assessments are confidential — federation not directly affected
R v Foreign Secretary ex parte WDM [1995]✅ Pressure group had sufficient interestRaised a serious legal issue; no one else could bring the case
R (Miller) v Secretary of State [2017]✅ "Concerned citizen" Gina Miller had standingRaised a constitutional issue of general public importance

Step 3: Public Body Only

JR lies only against a person or body exercising a public function.

CaseHeldPrinciple
R v City Panel on Takeover and Mergers [1987]✅ Public function — JR availablePanel regulated the Stock Exchange as part of the government's regulatory system — public function even though not statutory
R v Jockey Club [1993]❌ NOT a public body — JR NOT availableJockey Club was not created by statute, had no statutory powers, and was not part of any government regulatory system — only suable for breach of contract
Exam Tip: The test is whether the body performs a public function, not whether it is a "government department". Private bodies exercising public functions (e.g., the Takeover Panel) CAN be judicially reviewed.

Excluding Judicial Review

An Act of Parliament may attempt to prevent JR of a decision. However, the courts strongly resist such exclusion — they interpret the Act in an "extremely cunning way" to evade the restriction, because excluding JR restricts access to justice.

Anisminic v Foreign Compensation Commission [1969] 2 AC 147
Facts: The Foreign Compensation Act 1950 stated that a "determination" by the FCC "shall not be called in question in any court of law."
Held: If the FCC's "determination" was ultra vires (beyond its powers), it was not a valid "determination" at all. The wording of the Act only prevented review of a valid "determination" — it did NOT prevent review of a purported or incorrect determination.
Significance: A masterful interpretation that preserved judicial review despite apparently exclusionary language.

📖 Part 3: The Grounds of Judicial Review — Lord Diplock's Framework

In Council for Civil Service Unions v Minister of State for Civil Service [1985] AC 374 (the GCHQ case), Lord Diplock summarised the main grounds for JR as:

  1. Illegality (违法) — The decision-maker misunderstood the law or exceeded their powers.
  2. Irrationality (不合理) — The decision is so absurd that no sensible person could have made it (Wednesbury unreasonableness).
  3. Procedural Impropriety (程序不当) — Failure to follow procedural rules or natural justice.
  4. Proportionality (相称性) — Added later for ECHR and EU law cases (now via HRA 1998 s6).

📖 Part 3(a): Illegality (违法)

Illegality means the decision-maker did not have the legal power to make that decision, or misunderstood the law regulating their decision-making power.

Sub-categories of Illegality

1. Misunderstanding the Law

The court interprets the wording of the Act of Parliament to decide what decisions are allowed. The statute must be read in the context of general constitutional principles.

Padfield v Minister of Agriculture [1968] AC 997
Facts: The minister had power to regulate milk prices and could establish a committee "if the minister in any case so directs." The wording seemed to give absolute discretion.
Held: The minister could NOT ignore regional price disparities. The court issued a mandatory order compelling him to constitute the committee.
Principle: Even apparently absolute discretion must be exercised consistently with the purpose of the Act.
R (Unison) v Lord Chancellor [2017] UKSC 51
Facts: The Lord Chancellor set excessively high employment tribunal fees under a statutory power to "set fees".
Held: Although the wording seemed to give a general power, it did NOT allow fees that denied access to justice — a basic right required by the Rule of Law.
Principle: Statutory powers must be interpreted in light of constitutional principles (here, access to courts).

2. Improper Purpose

A statutory power must NOT be used for a purpose for which it was not intended.

Porter v Magill [2002] 2 AC 357
Facts: A council used its statutory power to sell council houses — but used it to encourage buyers to vote for them in elections.
Held: This was an improper purpose. The power to sell council houses exists for housing policy purposes, NOT for electoral advantage.

3. Relevant and Irrelevant Considerations

The decision-maker must take into account all relevant considerations and must NOT take into account irrelevant considerations.

4. Fettering of Discretion

The decision-maker must NOT "fetter" (restrict) their own discretion by applying a rigid rule or policy without considering individual cases.

British Oxygen v Board of Trade [1971] AC 610
Principle: A decision-maker MAY adopt a general policy, but must be willing to consider individual cases that fall outside the policy. A total refusal to consider individual circumstances = fettering of discretion.

5. Unauthorised Delegation

The decision-maker must NOT delegate their decision-making power to another person unless the statute expressly permits it.

6. Error of Fact

Traditionally, errors of fact (as opposed to errors of law) could NOT be challenged by JR. However, this rule has been relaxed — if the error is jurisdictional or the decision-maker made a wrong factual assumption that goes to the heart of the decision, JR may lie.

📖 Part 3(b): Irrationality (Wednesbury Unreasonableness)

Irrationality means the decision is so outrageous in its defiance of logic or accepted moral standards that no sensible person could have made it. This is also called "Wednesbury unreasonableness".

Associated Provincial Picture Houses v Wednesbury Corp [1948] 1 KB 223
Facts: Wednesbury Corporation licensed cinemas but imposed a condition: no children under 15 admitted on Sundays.
Held: Lord Greene MR stated: "A conclusion so unreasonable that no reasonable authority could have come to it." On the facts, the condition was NOT unreasonable (at that time, Sunday religious observance was common).
Test: "So absurd that no sensible person could have reached it."

What is "Unreasonable"?

CaseDecisionReasonable?
Roberts v Hopwood [1925] AC 528Council paid ALL employees (male and female) £4/week regardless of market rates (average £2.50)❌ UNREASONABLE — A socialist policy at excessive expense to ratepayers; not a proper exercise of discretion
R v Ministry of Defence ex parte Smith [1996]Ministry's policy: homosexuality incompatible with armed service⚠️ NOT irrational — Although judges personally disagreed, the policy was within the range of responses available to a reasonable decision-maker
Exam Tip: "Irrationality" is a very high bar. The court is NOT asking whether it agrees with the decision. It asks: could any reasonable decision-maker have made this decision? If yes → NOT irrational.

Human Rights Cases — Closer Scrutiny

In cases involving ECHR rights (via HRA 1998), the court will investigate reasonableness more closely — but it is still difficult to prove irrationality.

📖 Part 3(c): Procedural Impropriety (程序不当)

Procedural impropriety means the decision-maker failed to follow procedural rules laid down in legislation, or failed to follow the basic rules of natural justice.

1. The Requirement to Consult

An Act may require the decision-maker to consult interested parties before making a decision. The court decides whether the consultation requirement is mandatory (must be followed) or directory (optional).

CaseHeld
Bradbury v Enfield LBC [1967]Enfield HAD to give public notice before closing schools. Failure to consult = unlawful. Lord Denning ordered notice to be given.
Agricultural etc Training Board v Aylesbury Mushrooms [1972]Minister must consult "any organisation appearing... to be representative". Consulting ONLY the National Farmers' Union was insufficient — must consult ALL affected organisations.

2. Natural Justice (自然正义)

Natural Justice applies when a decision of a public body affects the rights of an individual. It has two basic principles:

Two principles of natural justice:

  1. The rule against bias (禁止偏见) — No one should be a judge in their own cause (nemo judex in causa sua).
  2. The right to a fair hearing (公平听证权) — The accused must be told the case against them and allowed to respond.

The Rule Against Bias

It is NOT necessary to prove actual bias. It is enough that a "fair-minded and informed observer" would conclude there was a real possibility of bias.

CaseBias?Explanation
Ridge v Baldwin [1964] AC 40✅ BiasCouncil dismissed chief constable based on newspaper reports of a trial. They had already decided he was unsuitable — incapable of giving him a fair hearing.
Dimes v Grand Junction Canal [1852]✅ BiasLord Chancellor Cottenham decided a case in which he was a substantial shareholder. Decision was quashed.
R v Bow Street Magistrate ex parte Pinochet [2000]✅ BiasLord Hoffman, a member of Amnesty International, sat on a human rights case where Amnesty gave evidence. The case had to be heard again without Lord Hoffman.

The Right to a Fair Hearing

What amounts to a "fair hearing" depends on the circumstances. The more serious the consequences, the more rights the claimant has.

CaseFair Hearing Required?Explanation
Ridge v Baldwin [1964]✅ YES — Full hearingDismissal of chief constable — required notice of charges and opportunity to respond
Lloyds v McMahon [1987]⚠️ Written hearing sufficientTax penalty case — less serious consequences; written representations sufficient (plus right of appeal to High Court)
Osborn v Parole Board [2013] UKSC 61✅ YES — Oral hearingParole Board decisions affecting liberty. Important facts in dispute → witnesses must be questioned. Oral hearing required.
R (G) v Governors of X School [2012]✅ YES — Legal representationTeacher banned from teaching — deprivation of livelihood. Legal representation appropriate.
R v Home Department ex parte Doody [1994]✅ YES — Duty to give reasonsLife prisoners must be told the reasons for their minimum detention period. General duty to give reasons unless justified not to.
Exam Tip: The key question for fair hearing is: What is the consequence for the individual? The more serious the consequence (loss of liberty > loss of money > minor penalty), the more elaborate the procedural rights.

3. Legitimate Expectation (合法期待)

There are two types of legitimate expectation:

(a) Procedural Legitimate Expectation

As part of natural justice, a claimant may have a legitimate expectation that they will be consulted about a change in rules or a decision affecting them.

Council for Civil Service Unions v Minister (GCHQ) [1985]
Facts: The minister proposed to ban trade union membership for civil servants at GCHQ. The council had previously been consulted about changes to terms and conditions.
Held: The council had a procedural legitimate expectation that they would be consulted. The minister should have consulted (though in this case national security justified skipping consultation).

(b) Substantive Legitimate Expectation

In certain circumstances, public bodies may be obliged to keep a promise they have made about their future policies.

R v North and East Devon Health Authority ex parte Coughlan [2001] QB 213
Facts: Coughlan and 7 other severely disabled patients were promised they could stay in their NHS accommodation for life. Later, Devon decided to close the facility.
Held: The patients had a substantive legitimate expectation. Devon MUST keep its promise. It would be unfair and an abuse of power to close. Devon could NOT show an "overriding public interest" that justified closure.
Test (from R (Niazi) v Home Secretary [2008]): (1) A clear and unequivocal undertaking given to a particular group/individual; (2) The defendant can defeat the claim by showing good reason for departing from the undertaking.

📖 Part 3(d): Proportionality (相称性)

Proportionality was identified by Lord Diplock as a ground that would "eventually become part of judicial review". It is now a standard ground in ECHR cases (via HRA 1998) and EU law cases.

The four-step proportionality test (see Lesson 2) applies:

  1. Is there a legitimate aim?
  2. Is the measure rationally connected to the aim?
  3. Are there less intrusive means available?
  4. Is there a fair balance between the right and the community need?
Exam Tip (Revision Tip from textbook): Proportionality is NOT a separate ground of JR in ordinary (non-HRA, non-EU) cases. It is part of the irrationality ground in those cases. But in HRA cases, it is a separate and explicit ground.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: What is the key difference between Judicial Review and an appeal?
JR checks the lawfulness of a decision (whether it was made legally). An appeal reconsiders the merits of the decision and the court can substitute its own decision. JR does NOT look at whether the judge agrees with the decision.
Q2: What are the four grounds of judicial review set out by Lord Diplock in the GCHQ case?
(1) Illegality — misunderstanding the law or exceeding powers; (2) Irrationality — Wednesbury unreasonableness; (3) Procedural Impropriety — failing to follow procedural rules or natural justice; (4) Proportionality — in HRA/EU cases.
Q3: A local council refuses planning permission to a building firm. The planning inspector who upheld the refusal is the brother of the council chair. Which ground of JR is most likely to succeed?
Procedural Impropriety — breach of natural justice (rule against bias). The planning inspector and council chair are related. A "fair-minded and informed observer" would conclude there was a real possibility of bias. (This is the fact pattern of an SQE1-style question.)
Q4: In Anisminic v FCC [1969], how did the House of Lords preserve judicial review despite exclusionary language in the statute?
The statute said a "determination" "shall not be called in question in any court." The House of Lords held that if the FCC's determination was ultra vires, it was not a valid "determination" at all — so the exclusionary clause did not apply. A purported/invalid determination can always be reviewed.
Q5: What is "sufficient interest" and how flexible is the test?
Sufficient interest (also called locus standi) means the claimant's rights or interests are affected by the decision. The test is flexible — it is more likely to be granted if the claimant has a strong case or raises an important issue (Miller [2017] — a "concerned citizen" had standing for a constitutional issue). It does NOT have to be financial.
Q6: Can a private body be subject to judicial review?
YES — if the body is exercising a public function. In R v City Panel on Takeover and Mergers [1987], the Panel (appointed by the Stock Exchange, not statutory) was subject to JR because it was part of the government's regulatory system. But in R v Jockey Club [1993], the Jockey Club was NOT subject to JR because it had no statutory powers and was not part of any government regulatory system.
Q7: What are the two principles of natural justice?
(1) The rule against bias — no one should be a judge in their own cause; test is "fair-minded and informed observer" (Porter v Magill); (2) The right to a fair hearing — the person must be told the case against them and allowed to respond. The more serious the consequence, the more elaborate the hearing rights.
Q8: In Coughlan [2001], what is a "substantive legitimate expectation" and when can the public body defeat the claim?
A substantve legitimate expectation arises when a public body makes a clear and unequivocal promise about its future policies to a particular individual/group. The body is obliged to keep the promise. It can defeat the claim by showing good reason (e.g., an overriding public interest) for departing from the promise (Niazi [2008]).
Q9: A council has power to grant taxi licences. It adopts a policy of "no more licences" and refuses all applications without considering individual circumstances. What ground of JR is this?
Illegality — fettering of discretion. The council has applied a rigid rule without considering individual applications. This is an illegal use of power. JR is the correct procedure. (This is the fact pattern of an SQE1-style question.)
Q10: When does the court NOT grant a remedy even if the decision is found to be ultra vires?
The court has discretion. It may REFUSE a remedy if: (1) the claimant acted unreasonably; (2) a remedy would now be ineffective; or (3) it is not in the public interest. Also, damages are NOT normally awarded in JR (only if there is a successful contract/tort claim alongside).
Q11: What is the Wednesbury test for irrationality?
A decision is irrational (Wednesbury unreasonable) if it is "so outrageous in its defiance of logic or of accepted moral standards that no sensible person who had applied his mind to the question to be decided could have arrived at it." It is a very high bar — the court does NOT ask whether it agrees with the decision.
Q12: In Padfield v Minister of Agriculture [1968], how did the House of Lords interpret an apparently "absolute discretion"?
The statute gave the minister power to establish a committee "if the minister in any case so directs" — wording that seemed to give absolute discretion. The House of Lords held that the power must be exercised consistently with the purpose of the Act. The minister could NOT ignore regional price disparities. He was ordered to constitute the committee. This shows: even apparently absolute discretion is limited by the purpose of the Act.
