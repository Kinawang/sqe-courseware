📖 Part 1: The UK Constitution — Overview

What is a Constitution?

Constitution (宪法) is the set of fundamental rules and principles by which a state is governed. The UK constitution is not a single document — it is composed of multiple sources.

Four Key Characteristics of the UK Constitution

CharacteristicEnglishExplanation
不成文宪法Uncodified ConstitutionNo single constitutional code; composed of multiple sources
不成体系UnentrenchedParliament can amend constitutional laws at any time through ordinary legislative procedure
柔性宪法Flexible ConstitutionLow threshold for amendment — ordinary legislative process suffices

Exam Tip: The UK constitution is often described as "uncodified" rather than "unwritten" — many constitutional rules ARE written (e.g., Magna Carta, Bill of Rights 1689), they just are not codified into a single document.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: What are the four key characteristics of the UK constitution?
Uncodified (not a single document), Unentrenched (ordinary process can amend), Flexible (low amendment threshold), No Supreme Law (constitutional and ordinary statutes take the same form).
Q2: State the definition of Parliamentary Sovereignty.
Parliament has the supreme legal authority in the UK to make or unmake any law, and no person or body has the right to override or set aside legislation made by Parliament.
Q3: What are the three corollaries of Parliamentary Sovereignty?
(1) Parliament cannot bind its successors; (2) All Acts of Parliament are equal in form; (3) Courts cannot question the validity of an Act of Parliament.
Q4: In Thoburn v Sunderland, what is the significance of "constitutional statutes"?
Constitutional statutes (e.g., Magna Carta, Bill of Rights 1689, Act of Union 1707, Scotland Act 1998, ECA 1972) cannot be impliedly repealed — they can only be expressly repealed by a later Act.
Q5: What did Miller v Secretary of State [2017] establish?
The government cannot use the Royal Prerogative to trigger Article 50 (Brexit) without parliamentary authorisation. The Supreme Court (8:3) ruled this would violate Parliamentary Sovereignty.
Q6: What are Dicey's Three Pillars of the Rule of Law?
(1) All government actions should be authorised by the law; (2) There should be equality before the law; (3) The courts would protect individual liberty.
Q7: What is the difference between Parliamentary Sovereignty and the Rule of Law?
Parliamentary Sovereignty focuses on where legislative power resides (Parliament is supreme). Rule of Law focuses on whether government action is lawful (law is supreme). On conflict, Parliamentary Sovereignty cannot override the Rule of Law.
Q8: Why is the UK said to have a "partial" separation of powers?
Because the PM and ministers must sit in the House of Commons (executive-legislative fusion); the House of Lords historically served as both legislature and supreme court; and the Lord Chancellor historically held roles across all three branches.
Q9: What was the significance of Anisminic v FCC [1969]?
It greatly expanded the scope of judicial review by holding that a tribunal's "decision" involving an error of law is not a true decision and can be declared void by the courts.
Q10: In M v Home Office [1994], what principle was established?
The executive must comply with judicial orders. A minister's defiance of a court order constituted contempt of court and a criminal offence — confirming judicial independence.
Q11: What is the difference between "implied repeal" and "express repeal"?
Implied repeal occurs when a later Act is inconsistent with an earlier Act, and the earlier Act is treated as repealed to the extent of the inconsistency. Express repeal occurs when a later Act explicitly states that it repeals the earlier Act. Constitutional statutes can only be expressly repealed.
Q12: How does the HRA 1998 limit Parliamentary Sovereignty without abolishing it?
The HRA 1998 allows courts to make a Declaration of Incompatibility (s4) when legislation is incompatible with Convention rights, but the legislation remains valid. Parliament decides whether to amend — sovereignty is preserved.
