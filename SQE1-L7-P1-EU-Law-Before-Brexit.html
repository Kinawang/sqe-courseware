📖 Part 1: The EU's Law-Making Powers & Direct Effect

Why EU Law Still Matters after Brexit

Two reasons why you still need to know EU law for SQE1:

  1. Retained EU law: EU law that applied in the UK before "exit day" (31 Jan 2020) remains in force as retained EU law (a new category of domestic UK law).
  2. The Withdrawal Agreement (TCA): The TCA between the UK and EU is directly enforceable in UK courts and must be interpreted in accordance with EU law principles.

The Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU)

The CJEU (formerly ECJ) interprets EU law and ensures uniform application across all member states. Before Brexit, any UK court or tribunal could refer a question of EU law to the CJEU. The Supreme Court was obliged to make a reference if a case involved EU law. The EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 ended this referral obligation.

Direct Effect (直接效力)

The CJEU devised the principle of direct effect to ensure EU law is obeyed and interpreted the same way in every member state.

Direct Effect: Claimants can enforce rights created by EU law in national courts (without needing to go to the CJEU). There are two types:
  • Vertical direct effect: The EU law may be enforced against a state authority (a "public body", "an emanation of the state").
  • Horizontal direct effect: The EU law may also be enforced against private persons and organisations.

Van Gend en Loos v Nederlandse Administratie der Belastingen [1963] ECR 1
Principle: Articles in the EU Treaty can have direct effect. If the wording is clear, unconditional, and precise, the article creates an individual right enforceable in national courts — even if it contradicts national law. Member states have "limited their sovereign rights" by joining the EU.

Directives and Vertical Direct Effect

Directives cannot be enforced against a non-state, private defendant. To enforce directives against private parties, the CJEU developed:

  • Indirect effect: Even if a directive does not have direct effect, the national court is required to interpret national law to give effect to the directive.
  • The Francovich principle (损害赔偿): In Francovich v Italian State [1992], the CJEU ordered a state to pay damages to individuals injured by its failure to implement a directive. This has been extended to serious breaches of other types of EU law (regulations, articles) that injure individuals.
Francovich v Italian State [1992] IRLR 84
Facts: Italy failed to implement an EU directive on employee protection in insolvency. Employees lost money.
Held: A state must pay damages to individuals for injury caused by failure to implement a directive. This is now a general principle: if a state commits a serious breach of EU law causing damage to an individual, the state must compensate.
⚠️ SQE1 note: The EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 s4 states that the right to damages under Francovich ceased to apply on or after IP completion day.

Supremacy of EU Law (欧盟法至上性)

Costa v ENEL [1964] ECR 585
Principle: Member states have made "partial transfers of sovereignty" to the EU and therefore cannot make laws incompatible with EU law. EU law has supremacy over national law. National courts must set aside conflicting national laws.
Significance: This is the foundation of the conflict between EU law and parliamentary sovereignty in the UK (resolved by Factortame).

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: What is the difference between "exit day" and "IP completion day"?
Exit day = 31 Jan 2020: The UK ceased to be a member of the EU. IP completion day = 31 Dec 2020: The implementation period ended; EU law ceased to have effect in the UK. EU law made before 31 Jan 2020 becomes retained EU law; EU law made between 31 Jan and 31 Dec 2020 also remained in force until IP completion day.
Q2: Can a directive be enforced against a private employer? Which case established this?
NO. Directives have vertical direct effect ONLY — they can only be enforced against a public body ("an emanation of the state"). They cannot be enforced against a private employer. However, the court must use indirect effect — interpret domestic law to give effect to the directive. (This is the fact pattern of an SQE1-style question.)
Q3: What did the EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 s5 provide about the supremacy of EU law?
s5 states that the principle of the supremacy of EU law does not apply to any enactment passed after exit day. However, for existing law (retained EU law), the supremacy principle continues where retained EU law conflicts with UK law made before exit day. Retained EU law wins over pre-exit-day UK law; post-exit-day UK law wins over retained EU law.
Q4: Can UK courts still refer questions to the CJEU after Brexit? Are they bound by post-Brexit CJEU decisions?
NO. Under s6 of the 2018 Act, UK courts cannot refer cases to the CJEU, and are not bound by CJEU decisions made on or after IP completion day. However, they "may have regard to" (may consider) post-Brexit CJEU decisions to help interpret retained EU law. The Supreme Court is NOT bound by any retained EU case law (can depart using 1966 Practice Statement).
Q5: A client's equal pay claim relies on an EU directive that gives her greater rights than UK legislation. Can she rely on it after Brexit?
The directive only applies in the UK (as retained EU law) if the CJEU decided it had direct effect BEFORE exit day. If it did, it has supremacy over UK legislation if that legislation was also enacted before exit day. Also warn her: directives only have vertical direct effect — she can only enforce it if her employer is a state employer, NOT a private employer.
Q6: What is the Francovich principle and does it still apply after Brexit?
Francovich [1992]: A state must pay damages to individuals injured by its failure to implement a directive (or serious breach of other EU law). After Brexit: s4 of the EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018 states that the right to damages under Francovich ceased to apply on or after IP completion day (31 Dec 2020). It is no longer available for new claims.
Q7: What did the Supreme Court hold in Miller [2017] and why was it constitutionally significant?
The Supreme Court held that the government could NOT use prerogative power to trigger Art 50 TEU (giving notice of withdrawal from the EU). Because leaving the EU would change UK domestic law and alter rights of residents, an Act of Parliament was required. This reaffirmed that prerogative cannot be used to change domestic law (consistent with Lesson 4 on prerogative limitations).
Q8: Under the Withdrawal Agreement (TCA), how must UK courts interpret UK law? Does the TCA have supremacy over UK Acts of Parliament?
Under the 2020 Act, every enactment (past and future) must be read and given effect "subject to" the TCA requirements — i.e., UK courts must interpret UK law to be compatible with the TCA. If interpretation is not possible, the TCA would have supremacy over UK law — unless the UK Act was passed after 2020 and is expressly worded to contradict the TCA. (This is still untested in courts.)
Q9: Which courts are bound by "retained EU case law" and which are not?
NOT bound: Supreme Court (can depart under 1966 Practice Statement); Court of Appeal (can also depart). Bound: Lower courts and tribunals MUST follow retained EU case law. This is the same hierarchy as for domestic case law — retained EU case law is now just another category of domestic precedent.
Q10: Can delegated legislation be used to amend or repeal Acts of Parliament under the EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018?
YES — but only for a limited time (2 years after IP completion day). The 2018 Act grants ministers extensive power to make delegated legislation to deal with problems caused by retained EU law. This delegated legislation may amend or repeal Acts of Parliament — a rare and significant power. The devolved legislatures have similar powers within their competence.
Q11: Does the EU (Withdrawal Agreement) Act 2020 permanently limit parliamentary sovereignty?
NO. s38 reaffirms that "the Parliament of the UK is sovereign". The UK Parliament is allowing the EU to "legislate" for the UK for the limited purposes of the TCA, but can withdraw that permission with a later Act. This is the same temporary limitation mechanism as the ECA 1972 — Parliament can always repeal.
Q12: A case in a UK court involves a question about the interpretation of retained EU law. There is a relevant CJEU decision from 2023. What should the court do?
Under s6 of the 2018 Act, the UK court is NOT bound by the 2023 CJEU decision. However, it "may have regard to" (may consider) that decision to help it decide the meaning of retained EU law. The court must also follow any retained EU case law (CJEU decisions made before IP completion day). If it is the Supreme Court or Court of Appeal, it may depart from retained EU case law using the 1966 Practice Statement test.
