Part 1: Central Government and Accountability

1. The Prime Minister

The King is the head of state in law, but by convention, the Prime Minister exercises the King's powers (i.e. the royal prerogative).

Key Term: Prime Minister (首相)
The head of the UK government, who exercises legal powers in the name of the King. The office itself exists only by convention.

How the PM is appointed:

  • Appointed by the King, but by convention, must be the person who can command the confidence of a majority in the House of Commons
  • Usually the leader of the majority party, but not always (e.g. coalition governments)

PM's powers (all by convention / royal prerogative):

  1. Appointing Ministers of the Crown
  2. Forming the Cabinet
  3. Determining the structure of government departments
  4. Control of the armed forces
  5. Control of intelligence and security services
  6. Foreign affairs including international treaties
  7. Official appointments (e.g. peerages, honours)
  8. Management of the civil service (PM is also Minister for the Civil Service)
  9. Control of Cabinet meetings
  10. Establishing Cabinet committees

2. Ministers of the Crown (政府大臣)

Government ministers are by convention chosen from members of the House of Commons and the House of Lords, from the majority party or governing coalition.

Hierarchy:

  • Cabinet: ~20+ senior ministers; the principal coordinating and decision-making body of government
  • Secretary of State: Highest-ranking ministers, all Cabinet members, each leading a government department
  • Minister of State: Middle-ranking ministers
  • Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State: Most junior ministers
  • Total: ~100 ministers
Key Term: Government Minister
A person who manages government business, chosen by the PM from MPs and members of the House of Lords.

3. Ministerial Responsibility (大臣责任)

Ministerial conduct is governed by convention, many of which are codified in the Ministerial Code — but it is not enacted by Parliament. The PM enforces the Code and may require a minister to resign for breaching it.

Ministerial Code — Key Rules

RuleContentCase
Confidentiality
(保密义务)		Opinions expressed in Cabinet and its committees are confidential; ministers must not leak themAttorney General v Jonathan Cape [1976] — convention is not legally enforceable
FoIA 2000
(信息自由法)		Cabinet proceedings and correspondence with ministers can be disclosed if courts find it in the public interest; the Secretary of State can override a court decision, but the court must accept the minister has reasonable groundsR (Evans) v Attorney General [2015] UKSC 21
Honesty to Parliament
(对议会诚实)		Information must be accurate and truthful; errors must be corrected as soon as possible; a minister who knowingly misleads Parliament should resign
Avoiding conflicts of interest
(避免利益冲突)		Must not accept gifts or hospitality that could create a conflict; must not use position for private gain
Probity (诚实正直)Covers private-life scandals, financial misconduct, criminal convictions unrelated to official duties, etc.
Practice Example 3.1: Profumo Affair (1963)
John Profumo (Secretary of State for War) had an affair with Christine Keeler, who was also involved with a Russian diplomat. Profumo denied the affair to fellow ministers, the PM, and the House of Commons. He eventually resigned.
Which Code rule was breached? Possibly a conflict of interest, but the clearest breach was lying to the Commons — a minister must retain the confidence of Parliament.

4. Political Neutrality of Civil Servants (文官政治中立)

The employment of civil servants is governed by the Constitutional Reform and Governance Act 2010. Although the Act provides that the civil service is managed by the Minister for the Civil Service (the PM), civil service recruitment is controlled by the independent Civil Service Commission.

Civil servants must be politically neutral and serve the government of the day.

5. Ministerial Responsibility for Civil Servants

Carltona v Commissioner of Works [1943]
Established the principle that a minister bears legal responsibility for the actions of their civil servants. Even if the minister is unaware of the specific work carried out by a civil servant, the minister must answer to Parliament for it.

6. Accountability to Parliament (议会问责)

How the government is held accountable to Parliament:

MechanismDescription
QuestionsMinisters and the PM must answer questions in the Commons. PMQs takes place every Wednesday. MPs may also submit written questions.
DebatesMPs can raise issues through adjournment debates and early day motions. 20 days are allocated to opposition day debates.
Select CommitteesComposed of backbench MPs, allocated by party proportion (government has majority). Committees elect their own chairs. Many are chaired by opposition MPs, including the Public Accounts Committee.

Departmental Select Committees — powers:

  • Each government department has a committee of 11 MPs
  • Power to investigate any aspect of their department's work and report to the House
  • Power to summon persons, documents and records, and appoint specialist advisers
  • The House can compel ministers, civil servants, MPs or members of the public to attend
  • The Ministerial Code requires ministers to ensure their civil servants are as helpful and candid as possible
  • Members of the public who refuse to attend may be punished for "contempt of Parliament" (reprimand only)
Exam Tip: Select committees CANNOT block a Bill!
Practice Example 3.2 is clear: departmental select committees do not approve Bills — they can only investigate and criticise. Whether a Bill passes depends on the parliamentary majority.
📋 Part 1 Summary
  • UK central government = King + PM + ministers + civil servants
  • Accountability = the central government must explain and justify its actions to Parliament
  • Purpose = ensuring government is controlled by MPs who represent voters

Part 2: Devolved Institutions (权力下放机构)

Key Term: Devolution (权力下放)
The UK Parliament delegates some of its powers to legislatures in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. Power is not permanently transferred — the UK Parliament can take it back. This is not "sharing", because the UK Parliament remains sovereign.

1. Scotland

Scotland had its own Parliament until the Union with England in 1707, which is why its devolved government is the most powerful.

The Scottish Parliament

  • 129 MSPs: 73 elected by first-past-the-post in constituencies, 56 by regional party-list proportional representation
  • Elections every 5 years, unless two-thirds of MSPs vote for an early election

The Scottish Government

  • Led by the First Minister, usually the leader of the majority party in Parliament
  • Other ministers are appointed by the First Minister
Scotland Act 2016 s.1 declares that the Scottish Parliament and government are a "permanent" part of the UK's constitutional arrangements, and may only be abolished with the consent of the Scottish people in a referendum.
But! Remember: the UK Parliament is sovereign and can, in theory, repeal this provision. A statutory declaration ≠ a constitutional constraint.

Legislative Competence (立法权限)

The Scotland Act 1998 expressly reserves the UK Parliament's sovereign power to legislate for Scotland. The Scottish Parliament's legislative power is limited:

What the Scottish Parliament CANNOT legislate on:

  • Matters outside Scotland, or laws incompatible with the ECHR
  • The Scotland Act itself, HRA 1998, European Communities Act 1972, EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018
  • "Reserved Matters" (保留事项): the constitution (including the Crown, Union with England, UK Parliament, Scottish Supreme Courts, political party registration, foreign affairs, civil service, defence, treason); financial and economic matters, home affairs, trade and industry, energy, transport, social security, professional regulation, employment, health, media and culture, etc.

The Scottish Parliament CAN set its own rate of income tax.

⚠️ A Scottish Act that exceeds legislative competence is NOT law! Courts can suspend its effect until the defect is corrected.

Key Cases

CaseHolding
Axa General Assurance v Lord Advocate [2011]Scottish Act did not violate the ECHR; court deferred to the elected legislature
Salvesen v Riddell [2013]Scottish Act incompatible with ECHR (breach of property rights); Scottish Parliament and ministers must find a solution
Christian Institute v Lord Advocate [2016]Scottish Act incompatible with ECHR Article 8 (right to private life)
Imperial Tobacco v Lord Advocate [2012]Scottish Act restricting tobacco advertising fell under "health protection" (a devolved matter), not "consumer protection" (a reserved matter)

Scottish Independence

Scotland Act 1998 s.30 allows the UK to grant the Scottish Parliament power to legislate on reserved matters via an Order in Council. This was the legal basis for the 2014 independence referendum — the UK government authorised the Scottish Parliament to pass the Scottish Independence Referendum Act 2013.

Practice Example 3.3
Can the Scottish Parliament legislate to hold an independence referendum on its own?
No. "The constitution" and "the Union with England" are reserved matters. While a purely advisory referendum to "test opinion" might be lawful, the 2013 legal opinion was that the Scottish Parliament has no power to hold a legally binding referendum.

2. Wales

The Welsh Parliament (Senedd Cymru)

  • 60 MSs: 40 elected by first-past-the-post in constituencies, 20 by regional party-list proportional representation
  • Formerly the Welsh Assembly; now renamed
  • In 1998 Wales could only make secondary legislation; it can now pass Senedd Cymru Acts

Wales's legislative competence is similar to Scotland's, governed by the Government of Wales Act 1998 (as amended). It likewise cannot legislate on reserved matters or in a way incompatible with the ECHR.

3. Northern Ireland

NI's political particularity: Unionists wish to remain in the UK; Nationalists wish to join the Republic of Ireland.

Status of NI

Northern Ireland Act 1998 s.1 guarantees that NI's status is determined by a referendum.

Practice Example 3.4
Nationalists win a majority in the NI Assembly and pass an Act declaring NI independent and joining the Republic of Ireland, without a referendum.
The Act is invalid. The NI Assembly has no power to modify a UK Act of Parliament. Only the UK Parliament can amend or repeal the Northern Ireland Act 1998.

The Northern Ireland Assembly

  • 90 MLAs: 18 constituencies, 5 members each, elected by Single Transferable Vote
  • Elections every 5 years; two-thirds vote required for early election
  • Normally simple majority suffices, but some measures require "cross-community support" — a majority of both nationalist and unionist MLAs voting in favour

The Northern Ireland Executive

  • Must have both a First Minister and a deputy First Minister
  • First Minister from the largest political designation (unionist), deputy First Minister from the second largest (nationalist)
  • Both must exercise their functions jointly
  • 10 other ministers appointed by the Assembly on a party-proportional basis
Exam Tip: NI appoints ministers differently from the UK, Scotland and Wales!
In the UK/Scotland/Wales, the PM/First Minister nominates ministers and the King appoints them. In NI, political parties nominate ministers. This distinction has been tested in SQE1 (Practice Question 4, answer D).

Legislative Competence

NI has the most limited legislative competence, distinct from Scotland and Wales:

What the NI Assembly CANNOT legislate on:

  • Matters outside NI, laws incompatible with the ECHR, or laws discriminating on the basis of religious belief or political opinion
  • HRA 1998, European Communities Act 1972, EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018
  • "Excepted Matters" (除外事项): cannot be transferred — Crown, UK Parliament, international relations, defence, taxation, immigration, honours, treason, national security
  • "Reserved Matters" (保留事项): could potentially be transferred in future — financial services, pensions, international trade, competition law, consumer safety, telecommunications, data protection, intellectual property, terrorism-related criminal law
⚠️ The three devolved legislatures do NOT have identical legislative competence!
The common ground: none can legislate on specified matters, and none can override UK Acts of Parliament.

4. EU Withdrawal and Devolution

Legislative Consent Motions — Sewel Convention

Scotland Act 2016 s.2 and Wales Act 2016 provide that the UK Parliament "recognises" that it will not normally legislate on devolved matters without the consent of the Scottish/Welsh Parliament.

When the UK Parliament legislates on a devolved matter, the relevant devolved legislature must draft a Legislative Consent Motion.

R (Miller) v Secretary of State for Exiting the EU [2017] UKSC 5
Held: The Sewel Convention is merely a "recognition" of a constitutional convention and cannot be legally enforced. Even though Brexit affected devolved powers, devolved legislatures have no legal veto. The UK Parliament is sovereign on this matter.
📋 Part 2 Summary
  • Devolved institutions = the governments and legislatures of Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland
  • What they do = exercise governmental and legislative power for their nation/region
  • Who controls them = the UK Parliament — power is granted by the UK Parliament and can be taken back

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: A businessman who is not an MP or Lord is appointed as Secretary of State. Is this valid?
Legally yes, but by convention no — a secretary of state must be a member of either House of Parliament so that they can be accountable to Parliament. (Answer: A)
Q2: A civil servant in the Department of Health makes an error that causes harm to a patient. Who bears legal responsibility?
The government minister in charge of the department — Carltona principle. The minister takes legal responsibility for what happens in their department. (Answer: C)
Q3: A minister has breached the Ministerial Code. Which breach requires mandatory resignation?
Knowingly misleading Parliament. Other breaches are at the prime minister's discretion, but this is the one that requires resignation. (Answer: E)
Q4: The first minister of Northern Ireland wishes to appoint a member of his party, John, as a minister. What is the procedure?
A political party in the Northern Ireland Assembly nominates John. This is different from the UK, Scotland and Wales where the PM/first minister nominates. (Answer: D)
Q5: The Scottish Parliament passes an Act (by simple majority) declaring Scotland is independent. What is the effect?
The Act would not be law because it is outside the legislative competence of the Scottish Parliament. The "constitution" and the "Union with England" are reserved matters. (Answer: B)
Q6: Can the Scottish Parliament hold an independence referendum on its own?
No. Under s.30 of the Scotland Act 1998, only a UK Order in Council can grant the Scottish Parliament power to legislate on a reserved matter (like the constitution). The 2014 referendum was authorised by the UK government.
Q7: What is the Sewel Convention and is it legally enforceable?
It is the convention that the UK Parliament will not normally legislate on devolved matters without the consent of the relevant devolved legislature. It is NOT legally enforceable — confirmed by Miller (2017). The UK Parliament remains sovereign.
Q8: What is the key difference between "excepted matters" (Northern Ireland) and "reserved matters"?
Excepted matters CANNOT be transferred to the Northern Ireland Assembly (e.g. Crown, defence, immigration). Reserved matters COULD potentially be transferred in the future (e.g. financial services, consumer safety).
Q9: What is "cross-community support" in Northern Ireland?
Some measures in the Northern Ireland Assembly require a majority of both nationalist and unionist members to vote in favour — not just a simple majority of all members.
Q10: Can a departmental select committee block a Bill from becoming law?
No. Departmental select committees investigate and report, but they do not approve Bills. It is up to the majority in Parliament whether a Bill becomes law.
