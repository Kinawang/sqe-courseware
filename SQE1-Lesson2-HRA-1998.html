📖 Part 2: HRA 1998 Core Mechanisms

Section-by-Section Overview (教材 Chapter 8 重点)

SectionMechanismKey Points
s2Interpretation of Convention RightsUK courts must "take into account" EctHR case law. Normally should follow (Alconbury), but may depart where no EctHR decision / conflict / misunderstanding of UK law. Lower courts must follow higher UK court precedents.
s3Interpretation of LegislationCourts must read legislation compatibly with Convention rights "so far as possible". May depart from literal meaning, "read down" or add words (Ghaidan v Godin-Mendoza [2004]). If impossible, primary legislation remains law, but subordinate legislation can be declared invalid.
s4Declaration of Incompatibility (不一致宣告)High Court and above may issue a declaration of incompatibility, but the law remains valid. Parliament may amend via ordinary legislation or s10 fast-track procedure (remedial order / delegated legislation). Early case: R(Anderson) v Home Secretary [2002].
s6Public Authorities (公共机构)"It is unlawful for a public authority to act in a way which is incompatible with a Convention right." Public authority = performs functions of a public nature. Courts and tribunals are also public authorities.
s7ProceedingsOnly a "victim" (personally affected) may bring a claim. Usually via judicial review, 1-year time limit. Pressure groups cannot sue as victims (NIHRC v re abortion law [2015]).
s8Remedies (救济)Full range of remedies available. Damages are modest — only for physical/financial damage or lost opportunity. EctHR favours "just satisfaction" (R(Greenfield) [2005]).
⚠️ Warning: s4 Declaration of Incompatibility does NOT invalidate the law — this is the key distinction from constitutional review in other systems (e.g., US). It preserves Parliamentary Sovereignty while flagging the incompatibility.

📖 Part 3: Proportionality Test (相称性检验)

The Proportionality Test (教材 P186) is the structured analysis for determining whether a restriction on a qualified right is justified:

StepQuestion
1Is there a legitimate aim? (Usually one of the exceptions listed in Art 8–11)
2Would the restriction achieve that aim?
3Is there a less intrusive restriction available?
4Is there a fair balance between the right and the community need?

📖 Part 4: Absolute Rights

Article 2 — Right to Life (生命权)

"No one shall be deprived of his life intentionally save in the execution of a sentence of a court following his conviction of a crime for which this penalty is provided by law."

Lawful Deprivation of Life (Art 2(2))

CircumstanceEnglishExample
Lawful killingLawful killing in self-defencePolice acting in self-defence
Law enforcementLawful arrest or preventing escapeReasonable force during lawful arrest
Quelling a riotQuelling a riot or insurrectionSuppressing a violent uprising
Death sentenceExecution of a death sentenceUK has abolished the death penalty (Protocol 13)
⚠️ Warning: "Detention of a person of unsound mind" belongs to Art 5(1)(e) (deprivation of liberty), NOT Art 2 (deprivation of life). This is a common exam trap.

Positive Obligation (积极义务)

The state has not only a negative duty not to take life, but also a positive obligation:

  • Protect against known threats to life (Osman v UK [1998] — failure to protect violated Art 2)
  • Establish effective legal and enforcement mechanisms to prevent violence

Article 3 — Prohibition of Torture (禁止酷刑)

"No one shall be subjected to torture or to inhuman or degrading treatment or punishment."

Three Forms (ascending severity)

FormEnglishDistinction
Degrading Treatment贬损性待遇Extremely humiliating, undermining human dignity
Inhuman Treatment不人道待遇Causes mental or physical suffering (not necessarily deliberate)
Torture酷刑Deliberate infliction of severe suffering (with state involvement)
⚠️ ABSOLUTE RIGHT — NO EXCEPTIONS: Art 3 is an absolute right. It cannot be derogated from, even in times of war or public emergency. Even a terrorist suspect detained by the UK government is protected — the UK would still violate Art 3 if torture were used.
Chahal v UK [1996]
Facts: The UK sought to deport a Sikh separatist leader to India, despite the risk of torture there. The Indian government gave assurances that he would not be tortured.
Held: The UK could not deport Chahal. Once deported, there could be no guarantee that Art 3 would not be violated. Diplomatic assurances were insufficient to displace the absolute nature of Art 3.

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: Under HRA s2, what must UK courts do regarding EctHR case law?
UK courts must "take into account" EctHR case law. They normally should follow it (Alconbury), but may depart where there is no EctHR decision, a conflict, or a misunderstanding of UK law. Lower UK courts must follow higher UK court precedents.
Q2: What is the difference between s3 and s4 of the HRA 1998?
s3 requires courts to read legislation compatibly with Convention rights "so far as possible" — this can include departing from literal meaning or "reading down." s4 allows courts to issue a Declaration of Incompatibility when s3 interpretation is impossible — but the law remains valid. s3 is about interpretation; s4 is about declaring a problem Parliament must fix.
Q3: Why does s4 of the HRA 1998 NOT violate Parliamentary Sovereignty?
Because a Declaration of Incompatibility does not invalidate the law — it merely flags the incompatibility. Parliament retains the choice of whether and how to amend the legislation. Sovereignty is preserved.
Q4: What is a "public authority" under s6 of the HRA 1998?
A public authority is any body that performs functions of a public nature. It is unlawful for a public authority to act in a way that is incompatible with a Convention right. Courts and tribunals are also public authorities under s6.
Q5: Who has standing to bring a claim under s7 of the HRA 1998?
Only a "victim" — a person who is personally affected by the alleged violation. Pressure groups and organisations cannot bring claims as victims (NIHRC v re abortion law [2015]). Claims are usually brought via judicial review with a 1-year time limit.
Q6: What are the four steps of the Proportionality Test?
(1) Is there a legitimate aim? (2) Would the restriction achieve that aim? (3) Is there a less intrusive restriction available? (4) Is there a fair balance between the right and the community need?
Q7: Why is Art 3 classified as an "absolute right"? What does this mean in practice?
Art 3 (Prohibition of Torture) is absolute — it admits NO exceptions and CANNOT be derogated from, even in times of war or public emergency. In practice, this means the UK cannot deport someone to a country where they face a real risk of torture (Chahal v UK [1996]), even if the person is a national security threat.
Q8: List the six lawful grounds for deprivation of liberty under Art 5(1).
(a) Detention after lawful conviction; (b) Non-compliance with a court order; (c) Lawful arrest on reasonable suspicion; (d) Detention of persons of unsound mind; (e) Detention of alcohol/drug addicts; (f) Prevention of unlawful migration.
Q9: What are the three essential elements of a fair trial under Art 6?
Independent + Impartial + Established by Law. The tribunal must be free from external pressure, have no preconceived position, and have a legal basis for its existence.
Q10: Explain the three-step analysis for Art 8 claims.
Step 1: Is there an interference with private/family life, home, or correspondence? Step 2: Is the interference lawful (prescribed by law)? Step 3: Is the interference necessary and proportionate in a democratic society? The burden of justification is on the state at Step 3.
Q11: What is the difference between the "internal forum" and "manifestation" under Art 9?
The internal forum (inner beliefs) is absolutely protected — the state cannot force you to change your beliefs. Manifestation (expressing religion publicly) is a qualified right — it can be limited if necessary for public safety, public order, or the protection of others' rights.
Q12: What is Habeas Corpus and which Article of the ECHR is it linked to?
Habeas Corpus (人身保护令) is an ancient writ allowing any unlawfully detained person to apply to the High Court to challenge the lawfulness of their detention. It is linked to Art 5(4) — the right to challenge detention. The court can order the detaining authority to bring the person before a judge.
