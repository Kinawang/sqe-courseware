Part 2: Breach of the Peace (破坏治安)

Key Term: Breach of the Peace
An ancient common-law concept. All citizens and particularly police officers have a duty to maintain the peace of the realm. Breach of the peace involves violence or a threat of violence, including provoking others to violence.

1. Definition

Two aspects to the modern definition:

  1. R v Howell (Erroll) [1982]: "An act done or threatened to be done which actually harms a person, or in his presence his property, or is likely to cause such harm or which puts someone in fear of such harm being done"
  2. Conduct that has the natural consequence of provoking others to violence
⚠️ Breach of the peace = violence or threat of violence, NOT noise or anti-social behaviour!
This is a frequent exam trap. Excessive noise alone is NOT breach of the peace.

2. Preventative Action

Moss v McLachlan [1985]
"Provided the police honestly and reasonably formed the opinion that there was a real risk of a breach of the peace in the sense that it was in close proximity both in place and time, then the conditions existed for reasonable preventative action."

3. Police Powers

The police may do anything reasonable to prevent a breach of the peace:

  • Power to ban a procession
  • Power to disperse an assembly
  • Power to enter private premises
  • Power to detain people
  • Power to prevent people travelling
  • Power to remove provocative signs or emblems
Exam Tip: Statutory vs Common-law powers
Breach of the peace gives police powers that overlap with public order legislation. In exam scenarios, identify whether the question is about the statutory position (POA 1986 / CJPOA 1994) or the common-law position (breach of the peace).

4. Human Rights Limits on Breach of the Peace

CaseFactsHolding
Redmond-Bate v DPP [2000]Christian fundamentalists preaching attracted a hostile crowd; police arrested the speakers when they refused to stopArrest unlawful. Speakers had Art 10 ECHR right to free expression. Speech was lawful even if offensive — it did not tend to provoke violence. Police should have dealt with the violent crowd members instead.
R (Laporte) v Chief Constable of Gloucestershire [2007]Police stopped coaches travelling from London to protest at an airbaseUnlawful. Not enough evidence that violence was imminent. Police cannot pre-emptively stop travel to protest.
Austin v Commissioner of Police for the Metropolis [2009] UKHL 53,000 people (including non-protesters) detained for 7 hours ("kettling")Lawful. Police action was reasonable and proportionate, justifying restriction of Art 5 ECHR (right to liberty). Mass detention = "kettling" is permissible when proportionate.
Practice Example 6.2
A large anti-war protest is organised in City Hall (indoors). Police fear opponents will disrupt it. What powers do they have?
This is not a public assembly under POA 1986 (it is indoors), but police could use their breach of the peace powers to enter the hall. They should not stop the meeting but should attempt to control the potentially violent opponents.

5. Enforcement

Breach of the peace is NOT a crime, although it carries a power of arrest. The arrested may be freed without charge.

Binding Over (具结保证)

A civil remedy with a criminal standard of proof (beyond reasonable doubt):

  • The court must be satisfied that a breach of the peace has occurred AND there is a real risk of violence in the future
  • The court must identify the specific conduct the individual must refrain from
  • Usually does not exceed 12 months
  • Defendant may be asked to pay a specified sum if they break the order (a recognisance)
  • If they refuse to be bound over, they can be imprisoned for up to 6 months
Percy v DPP [1995]
Defendant repeatedly climbed the fence into a military air base to protest. She was not violent and there was no evidence her actions would provoke trained military personnel to a violent response. No grounds to bind her over.

Obstruction of a Police Officer — s 89 Police Act 1996

If a person refuses the reasonable direction of a police officer legitimately trying to prevent a breach of the peace, they commit the offence of obstructing a constable in the execution of their duty.

Duncan v Jones [1936]
Defendants convicted for refusing to disperse a public meeting when a police officer asked.

Obstruction of the Highway — s 137 Highways Act 1980

  • The normal use of the highway is for passage and repassage
  • Wilful obstruction without lawful excuse is an offence
  • However, small protests on the highway are lawful if the highway is not actually blocked — there is a right to protest: DPP v Jones (Margaret) [1999]
📋 Part 2 Summary
  • Breach of the peace = common-law power; violence or threats of violence
  • Police may take any reasonable step to prevent imminent violence
  • HRA 1998 limits: Art 10 (expression) and Art 5 (liberty) constrain police — but "kettling" can be proportionate (Austin)
  • Enforcement: binding over (civil, criminal standard), obstruction of police officer, obstruction of highway

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: A man plans a public procession but has not notified the police. What is the legal position?
Failure to notify is an offence that may be committed by the organiser. It does NOT give police the power to ban the procession or impose conditions — those powers depend on the senior officer reasonably believing the procession may cause serious disorder, damage, disruption or intimidation. (Answer: E — prosecution for an offence)
Q2: Police stop football supporters from walking to the station after a match, telling them to wait 1 hour for rival supporters to disperse. A man refuses and is arrested. On what basis?
Breach of the peace. Previous violence makes it reasonable to believe violence might recur. Police may take reasonable preventative steps, including temporarily detaining people, and arrest those who refuse. (Answer: B)
Q3: Two drunk men fight outside a pub. A barmaid sees them and is frightened. What offence?
Affray. Riot requires 12+ people; violent disorder requires 3+. Two persons fighting with a bystander (the barmaid) fearing for her safety = affray. (Answer: C)
Q4: A man holds noisy parties at his house over several days, disturbing neighbours. The neighbours ask the chief officer of police to stop it. What is the legal basis?
Causing harassment, alarm or distress (s 5 POA 1986). Breach of the peace requires violence, not noise. It's not a trespassory assembly or rave (the partygoers are not trespassers). Public assemblies don't need authorisation. (Answer: D)
Q5: Animal rights protesters gather on the pavement outside a shop. They are peaceful but the street is completely blocked. The police ask them to disperse; they refuse and are arrested. What offence?
Obstruction of the highway (s 137 Highways Act 1980). The normal use of the highway is for passage and repassage. When the street is completely blocked, the protest goes beyond reasonable use. (Answer: A)
Q6: What is the key difference between a public procession and a public assembly under the POA 1986?
A procession is a moving demonstration; an assembly is static. The practical difference: processions can be banned district-wide (with Home Secretary approval), while assemblies cannot be banned — only individual assemblies can be subject to conditions.
Q7: When can the police use "kettling" lawfully?
When the detention is reasonable and proportionate to prevent a breach of the peace, even if it restricts Art 5 ECHR (right to liberty). Confirmed in Austin v Commissioner of Police [2009].
Q8: Why were the speakers in Redmond-Bate v DPP [2000] not guilty of breach of the peace?
Their speech was lawful and protected by Art 10 ECHR (free expression). Although it attracted a hostile crowd, the speakers did not provoke violence. The police should have controlled the violent crowd members, not arrested the speakers.
Q9: What distinguishes violent disorder from riot?
Violent disorder requires only 3+ persons (not 12), does not require a common purpose, and is committed by those who use OR threaten violence (riot requires actual use of violence for a common purpose).
Q10: In R v Jones [2006], why did the defendants' defence of "lawful activity" fail?
They argued that preparing weapons for an aggressive war in Iraq was not lawful under international law. The House of Lords held that under UK law the activity was lawful — therefore the defendants had no defence to aggravated trespass.
